Part 1 · Theory

Defining the Digital Battlefield

Weight: 1 · ~1.5 hours

1.1 — What Is Cyber Terrorism?

Cyber terrorism sits at the intersection of two already complex concepts: terrorism and cyberattacks. RAND Corporation offers a widely cited working definition framing it as premeditated, politically motivated attacks against information systems intended to cause violence or severe disruption against non-combatant targets.

The key ingredients that make something cyber terrorism rather than ordinary cybercrime:

Element 1
Premeditated

Not accidental or opportunistic. Carefully planned with a specific destructive or coercive goal.

Element 2
Politically Motivated

Driven by ideology — not financial gain. This separates it from ransomware and cybercrime.

Element 3
Targets Non-Combatants

Civilian infrastructure: power grids, hospitals, water systems, financial networks.

Element 4
Violence / Fear as Goal

The attack is designed to cause physical harm, widespread panic, or political coercion.

Cybercrime = financially motivated (ransomware gangs, credit card fraud)
Hacktivism = ideologically motivated, typically non-violent (website defacement, DDoS (Distributed Denial-of-Service) protests)
Cyber Espionage = intelligence gathering without direct disruption
Cyber Terrorism = ideological + violent/coercive intent + civilian targets

1.2 — Information Warfare: Older Than the Internet

Information Warfare (IW) is the deliberate use of information — and the denial, degradation, or manipulation of an adversary's information — to gain strategic advantage. Propaganda leaflets dropped from aircraft in WWI were information warfare. What digital networks changed is scale, speed, and precision targeting.

Modern IW doctrine, as studied by NATO CCDCOE (Cooperative Cyber Defence Centre of Excellence), covers the following sub-domains. Click each card to expand details.

Sub-domain 1
PSYOP (Psychological Operations)

Manipulating target audiences' beliefs, emotions, and decisions. Social media influence campaigns are its modern form.

Sub-domain 2
EW (Electronic Warfare)

Jamming, intercepting, or spoofing electronic signals. GPS (Global Positioning System) spoofing near conflict zones is a documented example.

Sub-domain 3
Cyber Operations

Offensive and defensive actions in cyberspace — destructive malware, data exfiltration, infrastructure disruption.

Sub-domain 4
Deception

Feeding false information to mislead adversary decision-makers. Includes fake military communications and fabricated intelligence.

Sub-domain 5
OSINT Exploitation

Using publicly available information to build intelligence profiles. Tracking troop movements via geotagged social media posts is a documented OSINT (Open Source INTelligence) vector.

Sub-domain 6
Censorship / Narrative Control

Suppressing information or flooding channels with noise so truth cannot easily be located or verified.

1.3 — Threat Actor Taxonomy

IW and cyber terrorism are not committed by a single actor type. Understanding who does what is essential for threat modelling. The MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge) Framework catalogues known TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) across dozens of documented APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) groups.

Nation-State / APT

State-sponsored, highly sophisticated, virtually unlimited resources. Operate under strategic geopolitical goals.

Non-State Terrorist Org.

Use cyber for recruitment, propaganda, and cryptocurrency fundraising. Growing technical sophistication.

Hacktivist Collectives

Ideologically driven, loosely organised. Operations often blur into IW territory.

Insider Threats

Individuals with legitimate access who misuse it. Among the hardest to detect and attribute.

Landmark Case Studies

These four incidents are foundational knowledge. Click each card to expand the full case analysis.

2007
HIGH

Estonia: The First Nation-State DDoS

First large-scale cyber attack against a nation's entire digital infrastructure.

2010
CRITICAL

Stuxnet: The First Cyber Weapon

A worm designed to cause physical destruction to industrial machinery.

2017
CRITICAL

NotPetya: Wiper Disguised as Ransomware

$10 billion in global damages. The deadliest cyberattack in history.

2016
HIGH

IRA (Internet Research Agency) Influence Op.

Coordinated inauthentic behaviour on a massive scale.

What happened: Following a political dispute with Russia over the relocation of a Soviet-era war memorial, Estonia experienced a wave of DDoS (Distributed Denial-of-Service) attacks that paralysed government, banking, and media websites for weeks.

Technical signature: Coordinated botnets generating traffic floods, exploiting the limited redundancy of Estonian internet infrastructure.

Key takeaway: Even without destroying hardware, an adversary can cripple a modern state by degrading its digital nervous system. This event directly led to the creation of NATO CCDCOE in Tallinn.

Defensive lessons: DDoS (Distributed Denial-of-Service) mitigation, internet traffic scrubbing centres, international incident response cooperation.

What happened: Stuxnet was a highly sophisticated worm that targeted Iran's uranium enrichment programme. It caused centrifuges to spin at destructive speeds while reporting normal operation to operators.

Technical novelty: It targeted SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) systems — specifically Siemens PLCs (Programmable Logic Controllers). It used four zero-day exploits simultaneously, unprecedented at the time.

Key takeaway: Cyber weapons can cause physical destruction. The boundary between digital and physical security is gone. See ICS-CERT (Industrial Control Systems Cyber Emergency Response Team) at CISA.

Defensive lessons: Air-gapping critical systems, integrity monitoring of industrial software, insider threat programmes.

What happened: NotPetya was initially disguised as ransomware but was in fact a wiper — malware with no recovery mechanism, designed purely to destroy. It spread globally via a compromised Ukrainian accounting software update.

Attribution: Attributed to Sandworm, an APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) group linked to Russia's GRU (Glavnoye Razvedyvatel'noye Upravleniye — Russian Military Intelligence). Full technical advisory available at CISA (Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency).

Key takeaway: Supply chain attacks (compromising trusted software updates) are an extremely potent delivery vector. A state actor can cause global collateral damage far beyond the intended target.

Defensive lessons: Software supply chain integrity, code signing verification, offline backups, network segmentation.

What happened: The Internet Research Agency (IRA), a Russian state-linked "troll farm," conducted a sustained influence operation across Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, and other platforms — targeting US social divisions ahead of the 2016 presidential election.

Techniques used: Fake accounts posing as American activists, amplification via paid advertising, creation of divisive content around race, religion, and immigration, and coordinated inauthentic behaviour — the defining concept of modern information operations.

Primary source: US Senate Intelligence Committee Report on Russian Active Measures.

Key takeaway: Cognitive infrastructure is as attackable as technical infrastructure. Democracy itself can be a target surface.

Part 2 · Hands-On Lab

Three Exercises, Real Techniques

Weight: 2 · ~3 hours

Lab Safety: All tasks use open, publicly available, legal tools on public data only. Every tool listed is a suggestion — research and use any equivalent. Never probe systems without written authorisation. Creative tool choices (including tools you build yourself) are explicitly encouraged and rewarded.

EX·01 Threat Actor Mapping with MITRE ATT&CK
⏱ ~60 min +

Objective: Use the MITRE ATT&CK Framework to map a documented threat actor's known techniques and understand how cyber terrorism and IW (Information Warfare) campaigns are constructed technically.

Step 1 — Navigate to MITRE ATT&CK Groups

Go to attack.mitre.org → Groups. Select Sandworm Team (also known as APT44 (Advanced Persistent Threat 44), Voodoo Bear, or IRIDIUM — attributed to Russian GRU (Glavnoye Razvedyvatel'noye Upravleniye)). Read the group profile and note: what sectors they target, what their documented motivation is, how many techniques are attributed to them.

Step 2 — ATT&CK Navigator Heat Map

Open ATT&CK Navigator. Create a New Layer → Enterprise. Search for "Sandworm" and load their layer. Observe the heat map — highlighted cells are their known TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures).

Step 3 — Document 5 Techniques

From Sandworm's highlighted cells, pick any 5 techniques. For each one record:

  • Technique ID (e.g., T1486)
  • Technique name (e.g., "Data Encrypted for Impact")
  • What the technique involves in your own words
  • Classification: destruction / espionage / information warfare

Step 4 — Compare with Lazarus Group

Repeat for Lazarus Group (APT38 — attributed to North Korea). Pick 3 techniques. Where do the two groups overlap? What does overlap tell you about attribution difficulty?

Download the MITRE ATT&CK dataset in JSON format from github.com/mitre-attack/attack-stix-data and write a Python script that parses and prints techniques for a given group. Document your script in the report.

EX·02 Disinformation & Information Operations Analysis
⏱ ~70 min +

Objective: Apply OSINT (Open Source INTelligence) techniques to identify structural signatures of a disinformation / information operations campaign. You are not spreading theories — you are dissecting confirmed, documented cases.

The 4-Stage Disinformation Anatomy

Stage 1
Fabrication

Creating false content: fake quotes, manipulated images, invented news stories.

Stage 2
Laundering

Publishing through apparently credible channels so the content gains false legitimacy.

Stage 3
Amplification

Bot networks and coordinated accounts spread the content to target audiences.

Stage 4
Narrative Anchoring

Repetition until the message becomes part of the information environment regardless of truth.

EX·03 Attack Scenario Design & Defensive Planning
⏱ ~50 min +

Objective: Think like a defender by first thinking like an attacker. Design a plausible (fictional) cyber terrorism scenario, then systematically dismantle it. This is standard methodology at ENISA (European Union Agency for Cybersecurity) and CISA.

Step 1 — Choose a Critical Infrastructure Sector

  • Power grid / energy distribution
  • Water treatment and distribution
  • Hospital / emergency medical services network
  • Public transport coordination system
  • National election management infrastructure

Step 2 — Build a Fictional Threat Actor Profile

Invent a plausible (not real) actor. Assign: name, type (nation-state / terrorist / hacktivist / insider), motivation, capability level, assumed resources.

Step 3 — Map the Cyber Kill Chain

Using Lockheed Martin's Cyber Kill Chain or MITRE ATT&CK structure:

01
Reconnaissance
02
Weaponisation
03
Delivery
04
Exploitation
05
Installation
06
C2
07
Action on Objectives

For each step: what the attacker does, what technique type is used, what goal is achieved. No need to specify exact tools.

Step 4 — Countermeasures Per Step

For each kill chain step, propose at least one realistic defence. Reference where applicable:

  • NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology) Cybersecurity Framework
  • ISO/IEC 27001 Information Security Management
  • CIS (Center for Internet Security) Controls

Step 5 — The Single Weakest Link

Identify the one point in your chain where a single defensive action would have broken the entire sequence. Justify your answer — this is the most important part of the exercise.
