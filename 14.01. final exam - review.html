SECTION A — Comprehensive Recap

A.1 — Week 09 · Operating Systems, Malware, Networks and the Web

A.1.1 The Operating System as a Gatekeeper

Every device you touched today — your phone alarm, the campus laptop, the espresso machine if it has Wi-Fi — runs an OS (Operating System). From a security standpoint the OS is not a "platform you build apps on"; it is the gatekeeper that decides who reads which file, which process may speak to which piece of hardware, and which program is allowed to peek into another program's memory. Strip those decisions away and the machine is a swarm of code with no rules, which is exactly the state an attacker would prefer.

The reference frame for all of this is the CIA Triad, defined by NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology):

  • Confidentiality — only the right eyes see the data.
  • Integrity — the data has not been changed by anyone unauthorised, deliberately or otherwise.
  • Availability — the system is there when you reach for it.

Everything else in this course bolts onto those three goals.

Privileges follow the hotel-key analogy used throughout Lecture 9. A guest card opens one room (standard user); a housekeeping card opens a floor (service account / specific group membership); the general manager's master key opens every door in the building (root on Unix-like systems, Administrator on Windows). When an attacker with a guest card finds a way to obtain the manager's key, the industry calls this privilege escalation — and it is the goal of half the malware ever written.

A practical Linux check sits one line away: id -u. If the result is 0, you are root; anything else is a regular user. sudo -n true will exit silently if your account can already escalate without a password, which is itself a finding.

A.1.2 OS Hardening: Closing Doors

"Hardening" is the unsexy word for making a system less reachable from the outside. The CIS (Center for Internet Security) publishes free, version-specific benchmarks for Windows, the major Linux distributions, macOS, iOS, Android, and most major cloud platforms. Five techniques you must be able to name:

  1. Patch management. When the vendor publishes a fix, install it. The WannaCry ransomware outbreak of May 2017 used the EternalBlue exploit against an SMB (Server Message Block) flaw for which Microsoft had already released the patch MS17-010 two months earlier. Organisations that delayed paid in hospital downtime and reputational damage.
  2. Principle of least privilege. Hand each user and each running service only the rights it strictly needs. A web server has no business writing to /etc/shadow.
  3. Disable unnecessary services. Every running daemon is a port that can be probed. The print spooler on a database server is, statistically, a future incident report.
  4. Host-based firewall. The deadbolt on the room door, independent of the lobby's security guard. Even inside the so-called "trusted" LAN, packet filtering at the endpoint is now considered baseline.
  5. Logging and auditing. Without logs, you do not have an incident response — you have a guess. The minimum is timestamped records of authentication events, privilege changes, and outbound network connections.

Ports are the doors. Twenty-two is SSH, twenty-three is the unencrypted Telnet you should never see in production, twenty-five is SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol), fifty-three is DNS (Domain Name System), eighty is HTTP, four-four-three is HTTPS, three-three-zero-six is MySQL, five-four-three-two is PostgreSQL, three-three-eight-nine is RDP (Remote Desktop Protocol). A junior engineer who can recite that list by heart looks senior in the first ninety seconds of a job interview.

A.1.3 Malware: The Uninvited Guests

"Malware" — malicious software — is any program written to harm, steal, or open the door for further harm. The catalogue you must know:

Type What it does One-line analogy
Virus Attaches to a host file; spreads when that file runs. The flu that travels via handshake.
Worm Self-replicates across networks without user help. A rumour that walks the city on its own.
Trojan Hides as useful software to be installed willingly. A gift-wrapped box with a camera inside.
Ransomware Encrypts files and demands payment for the key. Someone re-keys your house and sells you the new keys.
Spyware Silently records keystrokes, browsing, files. A microphone taped under your desk.
Rootkit Hides deep in the OS to keep invisible long-term access. A secret room behind the bookcase that isn't on the floor plan.
Adware Floods unwanted ads, often bundled with free installers. Flyers stuffed into every pocket of your coat.

Most modern intrusions follow the Lockheed Martin Cyber Kill Chain: Reconnaissance → Weaponisation → Delivery → Exploitation → Installation → Command & Control (C2) → Actions on Objectives. Disrupting any one stage breaks the chain — the defender does not have to be perfect, just present at the right link.

The complementary catalogue is the MITRE ATT&CK Framework, which lists hundreds of concrete techniques (T-numbers) that real adversaries use. When you read a threat-intelligence report and see "T1059.001", that is ATT&CK's notation for "PowerShell scripting" — and it tells you something practical about how to detect the attacker.

A.1.4 Network Security and the OSI Model

The OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) Model, maintained by ISO (International Organization for Standardization), breaks network communication into seven layers. Threats and defences live at every layer:

Layer Name Example threat
7 Application SQL Injection, XSS, broken authentication
6 Presentation TLS / SSL stripping
5 Session Cookie / session-token theft
4 Transport SYN-flood DDoS (Distributed Denial-of-Service)
3 Network IP spoofing, routing manipulation
2 Data Link ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) poisoning
1 Physical Cable taps, evil-twin Wi-Fi access points

Four defensive categories anchor every junior interview answer:

  • Firewalls — packet filters with rules of the form (direction, source, destination, port, protocol, action). Open-source platforms include pfSense and OPNsense.
  • IDS / IPS (Intrusion Detection / Prevention System) — alerting and (for IPS) blocking based on signatures or anomalies. Snort, maintained by Cisco Talos, is the most-deployed open-source IDS in the world.
  • VPN (Virtual Private Network) — an encrypted tunnel between two endpoints over an untrusted network.
  • Network segmentation — slicing the network so a breach in one zone cannot stroll into the next. Watertight compartments on a ship.

Firewall rule tables are evaluated top to bottom, first match wins, with an implicit final deny-all. A misplaced "allow any-any" higher in the list silently neutralises every restrictive rule below it — a classic Friday-afternoon mistake.

A.1.5 Web Security and the OWASP Top 10

The web browser is the most-used application on Earth, which makes web applications the single largest attack surface. The reference list is the OWASP Top 10, published by the Open Worldwide Application Security Project. For first-year exam purposes, three classics dominate:

  • XSS (Cross-Site Scripting). Attacker-supplied JavaScript ends up running in another user's browser. The fix is output encoding and treating user input as text (textContent), not HTML (innerHTML). For production sanitisation, the canonical library is DOMPurify.
  • SQL Injection. User input is concatenated into a database query instead of being parameterised. Fix: parameterised queries / prepared statements, every time, with no exceptions for "this one's just for an internal tool."
  • CSRF (Cross-Site Request Forgery). An attacker tricks a logged-in user's browser into making a state-changing request to a site where they are still authenticated. Fix: per-request CSRF tokens, SameSite cookies, and re-authentication for sensitive actions.

Every web framework worth using ships defences against all three out of the box. The reason they still appear in breach reports is that engineers turn them off when they get in the way.

A.2 — Week 10 · Authentication, Applied Cryptography and Key Management

A.2.1 Authentication: "Prove it"

Distinguish three neighbours that students confuse forever:

  • Identification — "Here is my username" (a claim).
  • Authentication — "Prove it" (verifying the claim). This is Week 10's territory.
  • Authorisation — "Now that I know who you are, what may you do?" (permissions).

The industry packages these into the AAA triad — Authentication, Authorisation, Accounting — formalised by the IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) in RFC 3588 (Diameter), the modern successor to RADIUS.

Authentication evidence falls into three classical factors:

  1. Something you KNOW — password, PIN, passphrase.
  2. Something you HAVE — a phone with an authenticator app, a YubiKey hardware token, a smart card.
  3. Something you ARE — biometric (fingerprint, face, iris).

Combining two or more is MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication). Microsoft Security research has repeatedly reported that enabling MFA blocks roughly 99% of automated account-takeover attempts. It is the single highest-ROI security control a normal user can enable.

The protocols you must recognise on sight:

  • Password + salted hash. A unique random salt is generated per user; the database stores (salt, hash(salt ‖ password)). Identical passwords no longer hash to identical strings, and a leaked database is a pile of fingerprints, not passwords.
  • TOTP (Time-based One-Time Password) — RFC 6238. The 6-digit code that rotates every 30 seconds in your authenticator app.
  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn — the modern passwordless standard from the FIDO Alliance and the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium). The technology underneath passkeys.
  • OAuth 2.0 — RFC 6749. "Log in with Google / GitHub / Apple."
  • Kerberos — ticket-based protocol; the engine inside every Microsoft Active Directory domain. Named after Cerberus, the three-headed dog: Client, Server, KDC (Key Distribution Center).
  • SAML 2.0 (Security Assertion Markup Language) — XML-based enterprise single sign-on, governed by OASIS.
  • RADIUS (Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service) — the protocol behind most enterprise Wi-Fi back-ends.

A.2.2 Applied Cryptography in Two Families

Two families and one combined pattern matter for the exam:

  • Symmetric cryptography — the same key encrypts and decrypts. Fast, efficient, but the parties must agree on the key somehow. The standard today is AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), specifically AES-256 in GCM (Galois/Counter Mode), an AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data) scheme that delivers confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity in a single primitive.
  • Asymmetric cryptography — a pair of mathematically linked keys. The public key may be shared freely; the private key is guarded with one's life. Modern examples include RSA, ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm), and Ed25519.
  • Hybrid cryptosystems — every real-world system uses both. When you load an HTTPS page, asymmetric crypto first negotiates a short-lived symmetric session key, and symmetric crypto then carries the actual traffic because it is thousands of times faster. That is TLS 1.3 in one sentence.

The CIA Triad returns as the why of cryptography:

  • Confidentiality — only the intended recipient reads the message.
  • Integrity — the recipient detects any alteration, even one bit.
  • Authenticity — the recipient is sure of the sender.

Modern AES-GCM gives you all three at once. Older modes (AES-CBC, AES-ECB) require a separate MAC (Message Authentication Code) to achieve the same. AES-ECB in particular leaks plaintext patterns and is essentially never appropriate — if you see it in code, that is a finding.

Three more building blocks you must know by name:

  • Hash function. One-way, deterministic, fixed-length output. Avalanche property: one bit changes in the input, roughly half the output bits flip. SHA-256 (Secure Hash Algorithm) is the current default; MD5 and SHA-1 are broken for security purposes and survive only as legacy checksums.
  • KDF (Key Derivation Function). Turns a low-entropy human password into a uniformly random cryptographic key by running a deliberately slow hash thousands or millions of times. The names to recognise: PBKDF2 (RFC 8018), scrypt (RFC 7914), and Argon2 (RFC 9106) — the latter being the modern winner of the Password Hashing Competition. OWASP recommendations as of 2023 ask for 600 000 iterations of PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA-256 or Argon2id with sane parameters.
  • Nonce — "number used once". Reusing a nonce in AES-GCM is catastrophic — it breaks the cipher's confidentiality, not just its integrity. This is one of the very few "always" rules in security.

A.2.3 Key Management — Where Real Systems Fail

Bruce Schneier put it crisply: almost every broken cryptosystem of the last forty years was broken not by defeating the maths, but by mishandling the keys. The lifecycle has seven phases — generation, distribution, storage, use, rotation, revocation, destruction — and the canonical reference is NIST Special Publication 800-57.

Generation must use a CSPRNG (Cryptographically Secure Pseudo-Random Number Generator) — secrets in Python, crypto.randomBytes in Node.js, never Math.random(). Storage in production should use an HSM (Hardware Security Module) or a managed cloud service such as AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, or Azure Key Vault.

A PKI (Public Key Infrastructure) is how the world trusts a public key. A CA (Certificate Authority) — Let's Encrypt, DigiCert, Sectigo — issues a digital certificate that cryptographically binds a public key to an identity (www.example.com). Your browser ships with a list of roughly 150 trusted root CAs; any certificate signed by one of them (or an intermediate they delegate to) is accepted automatically. Revocation happens through CRLs (Certificate Revocation Lists) or, more efficiently, OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol).

The Diffie-Hellman key exchange — published in 1976 by Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman — is the single most elegant idea in security. Two strangers, both on the same untrusted network, can agree on a shared secret in full public view, because the eavesdropper would need to solve the DLP (Discrete Logarithm Problem) to recover either party's private exponent. The modern variant is ECDH (Elliptic-Curve Diffie-Hellman), used inside TLS 1.3 and the Signal Protocol. With short keys (typically 256-bit curves) it gives more security than 3072-bit classical Diffie-Hellman.

Quantum computers, when scaled, will eventually threaten both RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography because they can solve the underlying number-theoretic problems efficiently (Shor's algorithm). For that reason NIST has standardised the first post-quantum algorithms: ML-KEM (formerly Kyber, FIPS 203) for key encapsulation, and ML-DSA (formerly Dilithium, FIPS 204) for digital signatures, finalised in 2024.

A.3 — Week 11 · The Computer Security Software Stack

If the previous two weeks were about what you are protecting and with which maths, Week 11 is about with which tools. Computer security software is the toolbox — and the seven categories below stack into the "defence-in-depth" model that the NIST Cybersecurity Framework and the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) both endorse.

The principle: any single layer can fail; attackers rarely defeat them all in one go. A medieval castle had a moat, walls, a portcullis, and archers — your laptop, similarly, runs more than one defensive program.

A.3.1 Defence in Depth

The doctrine is older than computing — it comes from the military — and the concentric-ring drawing every vendor uses is decorative. The substantive idea is that every ring should rest on a different technology, so a single bug in a single product does not become game-over.

A.3.2 Antivirus and Anti-Malware

Antivirus (AV) is the oldest category in continuous use; the first commercial product appeared in 1987. Two detection flavours coexist in every modern engine:

  • Signature-based. Hash a file, look the hash up in a database of known-bad fingerprints, shout on a match. Fast and accurate for known threats; blind to anything new. Real vendors ship ~300 MB of signatures, updated hourly.
  • Behaviour-based. Watches what a program does — writes to an autorun key, injects into lsass.exe, encrypts five hundred files a minute — and flags anomalies. Catches new malware; produces some false positives.

Names to know: Microsoft Defender, built into Windows since Windows 7; ClamAV, open-source, shipped with most Linux distributions; commercial heavyweights such as ESET, Kaspersky, and Sophos. For a second opinion on a suspicious file, VirusTotal runs 70+ engines in parallel and is free for individual use. The EICAR test string is a harmless artefact that every commercial AV is required to detect — useful for verifying an installation is alive without ever touching real malware.

A.3.3 Host-Based Firewalls

A firewall is a traffic filter. Each packet is matched, top-down, against a list of rules of the form (direction, protocol, source-address-and-port, destination-address-and-port, action). Host-based means it runs on the endpoint itself, as opposed to a network firewall sitting at the perimeter. You want both. Each sees a different slice of the traffic.

Built-in implementations:

  • Windows Defender Firewall since Windows XP SP2 (2004).
  • macOS firewall under System Settings → Network → Firewall.
  • Linux typically uses UFW (Uncomplicated Firewall) or firewalld on top of the kernel's netfilter / nftables subsystem.

The default-deny rule at the bottom of the list is non-negotiable: anything not explicitly allowed is dropped. If you find a firewall with "ALLOW any any" at the top, the rules below it are decoration.

A.3.4 IDS and IPS

An IDS (Intrusion Detection System) watches traffic or logs and alerts when something looks wrong. An IPS (Intrusion Prevention System) is the same thing with permission to block the traffic on the spot. Mental model: IDS is the smoke alarm; IPS is the sprinkler that also goes off.

The trade-off is risk tolerance. A false positive on an IDS wastes an analyst's morning; a false positive on an IPS blocks legitimate business traffic. Production environments often deploy IDS in front of business-critical applications and IPS at the perimeter, where surplus aggression costs less.

Open-source defaults: Snort for network IDS, Suricata for higher-throughput environments, Zeek (formerly Bro) for protocol-level analytics.

A.3.5 EDR and XDR

EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) is what happened when antivirus vendors finally admitted that signatures are not enough. Instead of fingerprinting files, an EDR agent records everything a process does — every execve, every file write, every network connection, every registry change — and ships the telemetry to a cloud console where analysts hunt for patterns that look like attacks.

The killer feature is correlation across events. The chain "Outlook spawns Word → Word spawns PowerShell with an encoded command → PowerShell spawns cmd.exe running certutil to download an executable → that executable writes itself to a Run registry key" is innocent at every individual step but unmistakeable as a whole. AV would miss it; EDR catches it.

Names to recognise: CrowdStrike Falcon, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, SentinelOne Singularity, Sophos Intercept X. Open-source cousins: Wazuh, OpenEDR. The newer acronym XDR (Extended Detection and Response) glues EDR together with network, email, and identity telemetry in one platform.

A.3.6 Encryption Software and Password Managers

Two categories that share one cryptographic engine.

Disk and file encryption protects data at rest. If your laptop is stolen, the disk is gibberish. The mainstream implementations:

  • BitLocker on Windows (uses AES-XTS, anchored in a TPM (Trusted Platform Module) chip).
  • FileVault on macOS.
  • LUKS (Linux Unified Key Setup) on Linux.
  • VeraCrypt as a cross-platform open-source option, descendant of the discontinued TrueCrypt.

Password managers (KeePassXC, Bitwarden, 1Password, Proton Pass) hold a vault of credentials encrypted under a single master password. Both categories rely on symmetric authenticated encryption (AES-GCM or equivalent) plus a slow KDF to stretch a human-typed master password into a 256-bit key.

A password manager solves the human problem: you can have a unique, strong password for every site without memorising them. The trade-off is that compromising the master password compromises everything — so the master password must be long (a 4-to-6-word passphrase) and the vault should be protected with a second factor.

A.3.7 VPN and Vulnerability Scanners

A VPN (Virtual Private Network) is an encrypted tunnel. Your laptop encrypts each packet and wraps it in another packet addressed to the VPN server. The untrusted network in between sees encrypted blobs going to one address. The VPN server decrypts and forwards. Café Wi-Fi, hotel LANs, and airport hotspots — all places where a VPN turns observable traffic into noise.

The protocols you must know:

  • WireGuard (2019). Modern default. Small codebase (~4 000 lines), audited, in the Linux kernel since version 5.6. Fast.
  • OpenVPN. Older, still widely used in corporate setups.
  • IPsec (Internet Protocol Security). The standard for site-to-site VPNs between offices.

A VPN does not make you anonymous — the VPN provider sees everything. What it does is hide your traffic from observers on the local network and change your apparent source IP address. Useful, but narrower than the marketing suggests.

A vulnerability scanner asks a target host: "which services do you run, which versions are they, and do those versions have known bugs?" The free options include nmap with the vulners NSE (Nmap Scripting Engine) script and OpenVAS / Greenbone. Commercial heavyweights: Tenable Nessus, Rapid7 InsightVM, Qualys VMDR. All ultimately cross-reference observed banners against the public CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures) identifiers managed at cve.org by MITRE and indexed by the NVD (National Vulnerability Database).

Legal note that appears on every exam paper somewhere. Scanning a host you do not own or have written permission to test is a criminal offence — in Spain under Ley Orgánica 10/1995 article 197 bis; across the European Union under Directive 2013/40/EU; in Canada under section 342.1 of the Criminal Code; and in the United States under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. § 1030). The scanner does not know that, but the judge will.

A.4 — Weeks 12 & 13 · Blockchain, DApps and E-Commerce Issues

The last two weeks of the course step out of pure defensive engineering and into the systems-design questions that dominate modern industry: how do strangers exchange value online without a trusted middleman, what does it cost when they try, and what part of the e-commerce problem can — and cannot — be helped by these technologies?

A.4.1 Blockchain: One Paragraph

A blockchain is an append-only list of records called blocks. Each block contains some data, a timestamp, and a cryptographic hash of the previous block. Because every block embeds the fingerprint of the one before it, you cannot quietly modify an old block without breaking the chain of fingerprints that follows. The list is replicated across thousands of computers (called nodes) which agree on its contents through a consensus mechanism. Nobody owns the list; everybody has a copy; changing it requires convincing the majority of the network.

That is the whole idea. Bitcoin, Ethereum, smart contracts, NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens), DAOs (Decentralized Autonomous Organizations), Web3 — every elaboration on top fits inside the previous paragraph.

The four ingredients:

  1. Cryptographic hash functions — typically SHA-256 (the same primitive from Week 10), standardised by NIST.
  2. Public-key cryptography — every participant has a public address (account number) and a private key (the password they sign transactions with).
  3. A peer-to-peer network — nodes gossip with each other directly, the way BitTorrent shares files.
  4. A consensus mechanism — the rule that decides which new block is appended next.

A.4.2 Bitcoin in One Transaction

Alice in Madrid wants to send 0.5 BTC (Bitcoin) to Bob in Lisbon.

  1. Her wallet builds a transaction object (from, to, amount) and signs it with her private key.
  2. The signed transaction is broadcast to the Bitcoin P2P network. Within seconds, thousands of nodes worldwide hold it in their mempool (the waiting room).
  3. Miners pick up transactions and bundle them into a candidate block. To win the right to add the block, a miner searches for a nonce (a number used once) such that SHA-256(block ‖ nonce) produces a hash with a required number of leading zero bits. There is no clever shortcut. You just guess, billions of times per second, until you get lucky. This is Proof of Work (PoW).
  4. The lucky miner broadcasts the new block. Other nodes verify it (verification is fast — only the search is slow) and start building on top.
  5. After roughly six confirmations (about an hour for Bitcoin), the transaction is treated as practically irreversible. Reversing it would require re-doing the proof of work for every block on top, faster than the rest of the network combined — which is the 51% attack scenario.

No bank, no SWIFT, no clearing house, no business hours.

The original whitepaper — Satoshi Nakamoto's "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" — is nine pages and free to read at bitcoin.org. Read it once before the exam.

A.4.3 Proof of Work vs. Proof of Stake

  • Proof of Work (PoW). Miners spend electricity guessing nonces. Bitcoin uses this. Pros: simple, well-tested, mature. Cons: enormous energy consumption — Bitcoin's annual electricity use has at times exceeded that of medium-sized countries.
  • Proof of Stake (PoS). Validators lock up ("stake") a quantity of the chain's native currency as a financial guarantee. The protocol pseudo-randomly selects who proposes the next block, weighted by stake. Misbehave and the stake is slashed (forfeited). Ethereum switched from PoW to PoS in September 2022 — an event known as The Merge — cutting its energy consumption by roughly 99.95%.

The 51% attack threshold applies to both, but in different currencies — for PoW, an attacker needs to outspend the honest miners on electricity and hardware; for PoS, they need to acquire more than half of the staked currency, which makes the attack literally self-defeating because the stake collapses in value when the attack succeeds.

A.4.4 Three Flavours of Blockchain

  • Public — permissionless, anyone can read, write, or validate. Bitcoin, Ethereum.
  • Private — permissioned, controlled by a single organisation. Often a "blockchain" that should have been a well-designed database.
  • Consortium — permissioned, jointly run by a pre-defined group of organisations. Hyperledger Fabric, hosted by the Linux Foundation, is the dominant framework.

Useful rule of thumb when someone proposes "let's use a blockchain for this": ask whether more than one party must write to the ledger and whether those parties distrust each other. If the answer to either question is "no", you almost certainly want a traditional database with good auditing instead.

A.4.5 Decentralized Applications (DApps)

A DApp is an application whose backend logic runs on a blockchain instead of (or in addition to) a traditional server. The frontend remains HTML/CSS/JavaScript in the user's browser.

The architecture has three layers:

  • Frontend — normal web technology, often hosted on IPFS (InterPlanetary File System) for true decentralisation, or on a normal CDN (Content Delivery Network) for convenience.
  • Wallet — browser software (MetaMask, Rabby) or hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor) that hold the user's private key and sign transactions.
  • Smart contract — autonomous code deployed once to a permanent address on the blockchain. Anyone with an Internet connection can call its functions. Its state is stored on the chain itself.

Smart contracts are neither smart nor (in most jurisdictions) legal contracts. The accurate label is autonomous code. The dominant language is Solidity on Ethereum; Vyper is a Python-flavoured alternative; Rust is the choice for Solana and NEAR; Move for Aptos and Sui.

Every Ethereum transaction pays a fee called gas, denominated in gwei (one billionth of an Ether). Gas does three jobs: it prevents infinite loops (a buggy contract runs out of gas and reverts), it creates a market for block space, and it gives developers an incentive to write efficient code. The current fee algorithm is EIP-1559 (Ethereum Improvement Proposal 1559), which splits the fee into a burned base fee and a priority fee tip.

A.4.6 DApp Categories Worth Naming

  • DeFi (Decentralized Finance) — lending, borrowing, swapping. Aave, Uniswap, Curve, Lido. Live totals at DefiLlama.
  • NFTs and marketplaces — OpenSea, Blur. The 2021 NFT bubble was mostly speculation, but the primitive — provable provenance of a digital item — has serious uses in ticketing, identity, and IP (Intellectual Property) tracking.
  • Gaming and metaverses — Axie Infinity, The Sandbox, Decentraland.
  • DAOs — on-chain governance via tokens. MakerDAO, ENS DAO.
  • Identity and naming — ENS (Ethereum Name Service) maps human-readable names like alice.eth to on-chain addresses.
  • Stablecoins — tokens pegged to a fiat currency. The dominant USD-pegged ones are USDC (issued by Circle, regulated under MiCA in Europe) and USDT (Tether).

The famous cautionary tale is The DAO hack of June 2016: a re-entrancy bug in a withdrawal function drained roughly USD 60 million worth of Ether before the community responded with a controversial hard fork that effectively reversed the theft. The split chain that refused to fork is still live as Ethereum Classic (ETC). The lesson for future developers: smart contract security is its own engineering discipline. The OpenZeppelin Security Blog and Immunefi Bug Bounty platform are good places to follow real exploits.

A.4.7 Layer 1, Layer 2 and Bridges

  • Layer 1 (L1) — the base blockchain (Ethereum, Bitcoin, Solana, Avalanche).
  • Layer 2 (L2) — a chain built on top of an L1 to make it faster and cheaper. Transactions execute off the main chain and a compressed summary is posted back. The leading Ethereum L2s are Arbitrum, Optimism, Base (operated by Coinbase), and zkSync. The underlying technology is the rollup, which comes in two flavours — Optimistic Rollups (assume valid, allow disputes) and ZK-Rollups (Zero-Knowledge Rollups) (prove validity cryptographically up front).
  • Bridges — smart contracts that move assets between chains. They are powerful, and historically the most-hacked part of the entire crypto industry — about USD 2 billion stolen from cross-chain bridges in 2021–2023 alone, according to the Chainalysis Crypto Crime Report.

A.4.8 The Seven Persistent E-Commerce Issues

E-commerce (electronic commerce) is the buying and selling of goods or services online. The classical categories are B2C, B2B, C2C, C2B, and D2C (Direct-to-Consumer). Blockchain-enabled variants add P2P (Peer-to-Peer) marketplaces, DeFi-funded commerce, and NFT-anchored ticketing, but the classical five still constitute the bulk of the industry.

After thirty years of online retail the industry has converged on seven persistent pain points:

  1. Security — fraud, breaches, payment theft, Magecart-style supply-chain skimming, credential stuffing against accounts. The OWASP Top 10 is still the canonical defender's reading list. The British Airways breach of 2018 — 380 000 cards exfiltrated through compromised third-party JavaScript on the checkout — earned a £20 million fine from the British Information Commissioner's Office (ICO).
  2. Privacy — what data is collected, by whom, for how long, and under what consent. The dominant law in Europe is the GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), in force since 25 May 2018; official text at EUR-Lex. Breach notification within 72 hours; maximum fine 4% of global annual revenue or €20 million, whichever is higher. In Spain the supervisory authority is the AEPD (Agencia Española de Protección de Datos). The US equivalent is a patchwork dominated by the CCPA and its successor the CPRA, plus sector-specific laws (HIPAA for health, COPPA for under-13s).
  3. Trust — fake reviews, counterfeit goods, vanishing sellers. Defences include verified-buyer badges, seller ID verification, escrow payments, and provenance tracking through consortiums like Aura Blockchain Consortium (run jointly by LVMH, Prada, and Cartier).
  4. Payments — the "Buy Now" button hides a dance through payment gateway → acquiring bank → card network → issuing bank, each taking a fee, with total processing cost typically 1.5%–3% per transaction. Add Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) under PSD2 (Payment Services Directive 2) for online transactions above €30 in Europe, and the 1.4 billion adults the World Bank Global Findex counts as unbanked — for whom cards are not an option.
  5. Logistics — the last-mile delivery cost is often higher than the gross margin on the product. Return rates of 30%–50% in fashion are common. Watch for environmental rules from the European Environment Agency (EEA) becoming standard practice.
  6. Regulation — the EU's Digital Services Act (DSA) and Digital Markets Act (DMA), both fully in force since 2024, impose transparency and anti-dark-pattern obligations. VAT (Value Added Tax) rules for cross-border e-commerce changed on 1 July 2021 with the EU's One-Stop Shop (OSS) scheme. In the US, the Wayfair decision (2018) made every state's sales tax potentially applicable to remote sellers.
  7. Accessibility and inclusion — the European Accessibility Act becomes broadly enforceable for e-commerce on 28 June 2025. The technical standard is the W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 / 2.2. The five most-violated guidelines: insufficient colour contrast, missing alt-text on product images, form fields without labels, keyboard-inaccessible custom controls, and CAPTCHAs that exclude blind users.

A.4.9 Where Blockchain Genuinely Helps E-Commerce

To resist the marketing instinct, train yourself to answer the question: "which of the seven issues are you addressing, and how?"

Blockchain genuinely helps with:

  • Provenance and anti-counterfeiting for high-value goods (luxury, art, pharmaceuticals).
  • Cross-border payments without correspondent banks, using regulated stablecoins.
  • Loyalty programmes that interoperate across merchants without a central operator.
  • Decentralised marketplaces for digital goods where escrow can be coded.

Blockchain does not meaningfully help with:

  • Shipping a physical box. Logistics is logistics.
  • Human-written reviews. The trust problem there is who wrote the review, not whether it was altered.
  • Most fraud detection, which depends on machine learning over private data the platform must keep private.
  • Regulatory compliance that requires the right to erasure — directly opposed to a blockchain's append-only design.

If a vendor cannot answer the seven-issues question specifically, the solution is marketing rather than engineering.

Case 1

Case Study 1 — "The USB stick in the cafeteria"

Coverage: Week 09 (OS / Malware, §1.3) · Week 11 (Antivirus, Lesson 2) · Week 11 (EDR, Lesson 5)

Scenario

Wednesday morning. A classmate hands you a USB (Universal Serial Bus) stick and says "the lecture notes from yesterday are on here — Carmen had to leave early so she asked me to share them." You quite like Carmen, and the deadline is tomorrow. You also remember today's lecture on malware. You sit down at the library workstation, USB stick in hand, mouse hovering over the slot.

Question

Describe — step by step, as a junior security engineer would — exactly what you should do before you let any byte from that stick touch any file you care about. Name the relevant defensive layers, the public services you would consult, and the legal limit on what you may do if the stick turns out to be hostile.

Possible answer

The single safest action is not to plug it in at all and ask Carmen to e-mail you the notes. If you must process the stick, treat it as untrusted media and run the following workflow on a sacrificial machine — ideally a disposable virtual machine snapshot you can roll back, not your daily laptop.

First, the physical / OS layer. Plug the stick into a system whose disk is not interesting to lose, with auto-run disabled (auto-run is disabled by default on modern Windows and was never default on Linux, but verify). On Linux mount the stick read-only: mount -o ro,noexec,nodev /dev/sdb1 /mnt/usb. This kills the most common "the user double-clicked something" failure mode.

Second, the antivirus layer (Week 11, Lesson 2). Run the host's installed AV — Microsoft Defender on Windows, ClamAV on Linux — over the mounted directory. The AV will hash every file and look up the hashes in its signature database. If anything pings, stop and report.

Third, the second-opinion layer. Upload any individually suspicious file to VirusTotal, which runs 70+ engines in parallel. Two different products independently flagging the same file is a much stronger signal than one. Be mindful that uploading to VirusTotal makes the file world-readable to security researchers — if it contains personal data you must redact first or skip this step.

Fourth, the behavioural / EDR layer (Week 11, Lesson 5). Open one file inside the sandbox VM and watch the EDR console. Outlook spawning Word is fine; Word spawning PowerShell with an encoded command line is the signature of a macro-malware kill chain.

Fifth, if it is hostile: isolate the VM (snapshot for forensics, then revert), notify the campus IT / SOC (Security Operations Center) helpdesk so they can warn other students, and — importantly — do not pivot and start scanning Carmen's computer or attempting to identify the source. Investigating systems you do not own is a criminal offence under Ley Orgánica 10/1995 in Spain, Directive 2013/40/EU across the EU, Criminal Code section 342.1 in Canada, and the CFAA (Computer Fraud and Abuse Act) in the United States — even when your intentions are good.

The unsexy but correct summary: physical isolation → signature scan → second-opinion scan → behavioural monitoring → escalate, never retaliate.

Case 2

Case Study 2 — "Café Wi-Fi, exposed laptop"

Coverage: Week 09 (Network Security, §2.1) · Week 11 (Host-Based Firewall, Lesson 3) · Week 11 (VPN, Lesson 7)

Scenario

You are writing a lab report from a café in central Madrid. The Wi-Fi is open — no password — and the captive portal asks for your e-mail. You notice your laptop is showing four other devices on the local network. One of them has a name like kali-rpi-001. You feel mildly uneasy.

Question

What defensive layers are protecting you right now (and what are not), and what should you change before continuing to work? Identify the protocols and tools by full name and abbreviation, and explain why each helps.

Possible answer

Three layers stand between you and the device named kali-rpi-001.

The host firewall is the first. Windows applies the "Public" profile by default when you connect to a network it does not recognise, which is stricter than the "Private" profile used on your home network. It blocks inbound SMB (Server Message Block, port 445), printer-sharing (port 631), and most RPC (Remote Procedure Call, port 135) traffic by default. If your neighbour is running a port scan or trying to mount your shared folder, Windows drops the packets silently. macOS firewall behaves similarly when enabled in System Settings. On Linux, UFW (Uncomplicated Firewall) wraps the kernel's nftables. Confirm the profile: Settings → Network & Internet → click the network → set "Network profile type" to "Public".

The operating system patches are the second. Microsoft, Apple, and most Linux distributions ship critical patches monthly. A laptop two months behind on patches is one of the most commonly exploited targets on a public network, because automated tools scan the wireless segment for known unpatched vulnerabilities (think SMB, RDP, browser extensions). Apply pending updates before you reach a public network, not after — by then it is too late.

The TLS layer is the third. Every site you should be using is HTTPS-only, and modern browsers refuse to load mixed content. If you only visit HTTPS sites, the open Wi-Fi sees encrypted blobs and can identify which sites you visited (via SNI — Server Name Indication, and DNS queries) but not the contents of those sessions. This is real protection but narrower than people assume.

What does not protect you. The captive portal is not a security control; it is a marketing data-grab. The café's Wi-Fi password (if there were one) would not protect you from other connected guests on the same broadcast domain — it only keeps outsiders off the network. Antivirus protects against malicious files, not active network attacks. The network is flat (everyone on the same subnet) and the router's "client isolation" feature, if present, is typically disabled at small-business locations.

What to change. Start a WireGuard VPN to a trusted endpoint — your university VPN concentrator, a commercial provider, or your own home router. The VPN encrypts every packet you emit before it leaves your network card and wraps it in an outer packet addressed only to the VPN server. The café sees a single encrypted tunnel; everyone else on the broadcast domain sees nothing useful at all. Even your DNS queries go through the tunnel.

The professional version of the answer adds: a VPN does not make you anonymous — the VPN provider sees the traffic you used to send to the café — and choosing a VPN provider with a credible no-logs audit matters. WireGuard is the modern default, OpenVPN is fine for legacy corporate setups, IPsec is the standard for site-to-site office links.

Case 3

Case Study 3 — "The 200-character password vault"

Coverage: Week 10 (Applied Cryptography, Lesson 2; Key Management, Lesson 3) · Week 11 (Encryption Software & Password Managers, Lesson 6)

Scenario

Your roommate proudly tells you that they have moved all 200 of their accounts into a password manager called PasswordVault. Their master password is Madrid2026. They are about to enable cloud sync so they can use the same vault on three devices.

Question

Identify every security weakness in this setup. For each one, explain which cryptographic primitive or principle is being violated and what the fix is.

Possible answer

Four problems, in roughly increasing order of seriousness.

1. The master password is weak. Madrid2026 is 10 characters with a predictable pattern (city + year). It is in every password-cracking dictionary that has been distributed since 2023. The role of the master password is to be the input to a KDF (Key Derivation Function) — almost certainly PBKDF2, scrypt, or Argon2 — which stretches it into a 256-bit AES key. The KDF is deliberately slow (the OWASP guidance as of 2023 is 600 000 iterations of PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA-256, or Argon2id with appropriate memory cost), but slow only matters if the attacker is brute-forcing the master password. With Madrid2026, the attacker does not brute-force — they look it up. The fix is a long, random-source-of-entropy passphrase, ideally 4–6 unrelated words generated with a dedicated tool (diceware lists exist for the purpose), or a fully random 16+ character string memorised once.

2. There is no second factor. If the vault is encrypted only with a password, then anyone who recovers the encrypted vault (a leak, a stolen laptop, a backup someone copied to the wrong USB stick) needs only to crack the password. Real password managers — Bitwarden, 1Password, KeePassXC with KeeAgent — support a second factor: a hardware token like a YubiKey (WebAuthn / FIDO2), a TOTP code, or in some products a separate secret key appended to the master password. Two factors mean "leaked vault + leaked password" is still not enough. The fix is to enable a second factor before turning on cloud sync.

3. Cloud sync without verifying end-to-end encryption. "Cloud sync" can mean two very different things. In a good design, the vault is encrypted on the user's device with a key the cloud provider never sees, and only the encrypted blob is uploaded; the provider could fail, be hacked, or be subpoenaed and the contents would still be unreadable. In a bad design, the provider sees the plaintext credentials. Before enabling sync, your roommate should verify the product documents an end-to-end encrypted ("zero-knowledge") architecture and ideally publishes a third-party audit. "PasswordVault" — a product not on this exam syllabus's recommended list — must publish an audit before any cloud sync should be trusted with 200 credentials.

4. The recovery story is missing. If your roommate loses the master password, the vault is unrecoverable by design (that is the point of a key derived from the password — no provider back-door). They need a printed recovery code, a sealed envelope, or a hardware backup token stored offline, before the day they need it.

Tying it back to Week 10: the master password is just human entropy; the KDF turns it into a 256-bit AES key; AES-GCM (or equivalent AEAD) encrypts the vault for confidentiality and integrity; the second factor adds an independent lock; sync is safe only if the cloud only ever sees ciphertext. Each layer addresses a different attack — and the absence of any one layer is exploitable on its own.

Case 4

Case Study 4 — "The deleted database — was it a key issue?"

Coverage: Week 10 (Key Management, Lesson 3) · Week 09 (OS Security, §1.1) · Week 11 (Encryption Software, Lesson 6)

Scenario

A small Madrid startup encrypts its customer database "at rest" using AES-256. Last weekend an attacker walked in through a phishing-installed remote-access trojan on a developer's laptop. Within an hour the entire customer database had been copied out and the attacker was demanding payment to keep it private. The developers are confused: "we encrypted it — how is this possible?"

Question

Explain to the developers in plain language why "encrypted at rest" did not protect them, and what they should have done differently. Use the seven-stage key lifecycle from Week 10 as your structure.

Possible answer

"Encrypted at rest" is the right phrase for a narrow protection: when the disk is physically lost or stolen, the bytes on it are gibberish to the thief. It is not a protection against an attacker who has running access to a machine on which the database is currently in use, because at that moment the system has the key in memory and is happily decrypting query results back to plaintext for any process with the right credentials.

The seven-stage key-management lifecycle from NIST SP 800-57 — generation, distribution, storage, use, rotation, revocation, destruction — exposes exactly where this attack landed.

  • Generation. Probably fine; AES-256 keys from os.urandom() or the equivalent are well understood.
  • Distribution. Here is where the rot begins. If the application server reads the database encryption key from a config file on its own disk, the key has been distributed to anyone with read access to that file. Including a developer's laptop, if developers keep production secrets locally for debugging. The fix is never to copy production keys to developer machines. Use environment-specific secrets, fetched at runtime from a managed service.
  • Storage. The key should live in an HSM (Hardware Security Module) or a managed equivalent — AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, Azure Key Vault, or HashiCorp Vault. The application calls the HSM to decrypt small bits on demand; the key itself never leaves the HSM boundary. Even an attacker with root on the application server cannot exfiltrate the key — only the ability to ask the HSM to decrypt while they hold that access.
  • Use. Apply the principle of least privilege. The application user that the web server runs under should be able to read customer rows it needs for the request in flight, not bulk-export the whole table. A query like SELECT * FROM customers LIMIT 1000000 ought to trip a rate-limit on first sight.
  • Rotation. Encryption keys should be rotated on a schedule — every 90 days for high-value secrets is a reasonable default. Rotation is itself the warning bell: if the only way you discover a leak is the attacker's demand letter, you are months too late.
  • Revocation. If a developer leaves, a laptop is lost, or a key is suspected of compromise, the revocation procedure must already be written down and rehearsed.
  • Destruction. At end-of-life, keys must be cryptographically erased, not "deleted" — overwritten with random data or destroyed at the HSM layer.

Tying it back to the attack: phishing → trojan → developer laptop → config file → key → "encrypted" database now decrypts smoothly for the attacker. The cryptography was fine. The operational handling of the key was the bug. This is exactly what Bruce Schneier means when he writes that almost every broken cryptosystem of the last forty years was broken at the key-handling step, not the maths.

The corrective steps for this startup: store the key in a managed KMS (Key Management Service), have the application authenticate to the KMS with a workload identity (not a long-lived API key), keep production secrets off all developer machines, enable database-level row-by-row authorisation, log every key-use, and rotate. Combine all that with the broader Week 9 hardening (MFA on all developer accounts, EDR on the laptops to catch the trojan earlier, network segmentation so a developer's machine cannot reach the production database directly) and the attack chain has five different points where it could have been broken.

The unhappy general lesson: encryption is a control, not a magic spell. It defends a specific threat model (a thief carrying off the disk), and a different threat (an attacker with credentials inside the running system) needs different controls.

Case Study 5 — "The phishing e-mail and the kill chain"

Coverage: Week 09 (Malware §1.3; Cyber Kill Chain) · Week 11 (EDR, Lesson 5)

Scenario

Monday 09:14. An employee at a small Madrid accountancy receives an e-mail from "ibe-ria-refunds[.]es" telling her she is owed €127.40 from a recent flight cancellation. She clicks the link, opens the attached refund.docx, and clicks Enable Editing. Three seconds later her screen flashes for a fraction of a second. She thinks nothing of it. Twelve hours later, the office's file server is encrypted and a ransom note is on every desktop.

Question

Map this incident onto the Lockheed Martin Cyber Kill Chain. At which stage(s) could a defender have broken the chain? Be specific about which tools from Weeks 9 and 11 would have intervened.

Possible answer

The seven stages map onto this incident as follows.

Reconnaissance. The attacker chose Iberia because flight cancellations are common; they probably scraped LinkedIn for a list of Madrid accountancies and bought an e-mail list. Defender intervention is mostly outside the firm's control: minimising data on public profiles helps; this is also where industry-wide breach databases like Have I Been Pwned become useful for monitoring whether a corporate domain appears in stolen-credential dumps.

Weaponisation. The attacker built refund.docx containing a malicious macro. The macro is the payload. Defender intervention: the firm could not see this happening, but knowing what tools are commonly weaponised informs the controls below.

Delivery. Phishing e-mail. Defender intervention: a mature e-mail security gateway (Microsoft Defender for Office 365, Mimecast, Proofpoint) flags impersonation patterns (lookalike domain, DMARC failure, suspicious link). DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance), SPF (Sender Policy Framework), and DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) configured on the legitimate iberia.com domain make impersonation harder. Training employees to hover over a link and check the domain catches a large fraction of phishing.

Exploitation. Microsoft Word opens the document. The macro runs because the user clicked Enable Editing. Defender intervention: this is where Office's Protected View and "Block macros from the Internet" Group Policy land. Microsoft has shipped these settings as defaults since 2022 — the firm should verify they were not turned off by some helpful but misguided IT decision in 2015 that never got revisited.

Installation. The macro spawns PowerShell with an encoded command, PowerShell downloads a payload using certutil.exe, the payload writes itself to a Run registry key for persistence. Defender intervention: this is EDR territory. The chain "WINWORD.EXE → powershell.exe with -enc" is a textbook macro-malware signature. Modern EDR products (CrowdStrike Falcon, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, SentinelOne) raise a HIGH alert and can auto-isolate the host. Without EDR, classical AV is unlikely to flag the chain because each individual binary is legitimate ("living off the land").

Command & Control (C2). The dropped payload phones home. Defender intervention: outbound network filtering — an egress firewall that requires DNS queries to go through a controlled resolver (with malicious-domain blocklists) and that flags new beaconing patterns. Network IDS / IPS like Snort or Suricata catches known C2 signatures. The C2 step is also when network segmentation pays — the workstation should not be able to reach the file server's SMB shares as administrator.

Actions on Objectives. The attacker pivots to the file server and runs the ransomware. Defender intervention: immutable backups (a backup the attacker cannot encrypt) limit the damage; the 3-2-1 backup rule is the right starting point — three copies, on two different media, with one off-site. Network segmentation slows lateral movement. Least privilege means a single compromised user account cannot reach every share.

The single most useful intervention. If forced to pick one: an EDR with auto-isolation between Installation and C2. By the time the file server is encrypting, the attacker has been in the network for hours, often days. EDR shortens that window from hours to seconds.

The single most expensive missed intervention. The 2022 Microsoft default that blocks macros from the Internet would, alone, have prevented exploitation entirely. Many firms turned it off because "the accountancy team needs macros". That is a workflow problem to be solved on the workflow side — not by disabling the highest-yield default of the decade.

Case 6

Case Study 6 — "Buying a printer with crypto"

Coverage: Weeks 12-13 (Blockchain Lesson 1; DApps Lesson 2; E-Commerce Lesson 3)

Scenario

A boutique e-commerce site in Madrid sells refurbished printers. The owner, who has been reading too much Twitter, decides to accept payment in cryptocurrency for the "international" customer segment. She is choosing between three options: (A) accept ETH (Ether) directly to a wallet she controls, (B) accept USDC (USD Coin) through a payment processor like Stripe that converts to euros automatically, or (C) deploy her own smart contract on Ethereum that holds payments in escrow until delivery is confirmed.

Question

Walk through each option's security, payment, regulatory, and operational properties using the seven persistent e-commerce issues as your structure. Recommend one and justify the choice.

Possible answer

The seven issues — security, privacy, trust, payments, logistics, regulation, accessibility — frame each option cleanly.

Option A — accept ETH directly to a self-custody wallet.

  • Security. The wallet's private key is now a single point of failure. Lose it, lose the business income. Have it stolen, ditto. There is no chargeback or recovery once a transaction is mined.
  • Privacy. Public blockchain; every payment is permanently visible. Customers see other customers' transaction history if they look. The merchant's address becomes a public business indicator.
  • Trust. No middleman to dispute. If the printer never arrives, the customer has no chargeback path other than reputation pressure on the merchant.
  • Payments. Gas fees can spike unpredictably on Ethereum mainnet. ETH price volatility means the "€450" printer might be paid for with what is worth €380 by Tuesday. Practically forces conversion to EUR immediately, which means a separate exchange relationship.
  • Logistics. Unchanged. The blockchain does not deliver the printer.
  • Regulation. The merchant becomes responsible for the accounting and tax treatment of every transaction (Spanish tax authority Agencia Tributaria and VAT obligations apply; in 2026 the EU's MiCA framework requires reporting). KYC/AML (Know Your Customer / Anti-Money Laundering) obligations may apply depending on volume.
  • Accessibility. High barrier to entry — customers need a wallet, ETH, gas, and the patience to confirm a transaction.

Option B — accept USDC through a regulated payment processor.

  • Security. The processor is a regulated entity with its own controls. The merchant never sees private keys.
  • Privacy. The processor handles PII (Personally Identifiable Information) under GDPR with a defined DPA (Data Processing Agreement). The on-chain transaction is settler-to-processor, not customer-to-merchant.
  • Trust. The processor handles disputes and is itself accountable under consumer law.
  • Payments. Predictable fees (typically 1.5%–3%). Settlement in euros within one to three business days. The customer pays in stablecoin; the merchant receives bank-account euros. USDC is issued by Circle under MiCA regulation in Europe.
  • Logistics. Unchanged.
  • Regulation. The processor handles the heavy lifting. The merchant follows their standard onboarding/KYC.
  • Accessibility. Reasonable. The customer needs a wallet with USDC, which is now within the reach of most cross-border-spending customers.

Option C — deploy her own escrow smart contract.

  • Security. The smart contract is now the merchant's responsibility. Bugs are exploits. The DAO hack of June 2016 drained USD 60 million through one re-entrancy bug. A small e-commerce site does not have the budget for the security audit a serious contract deserves.
  • Privacy. As in Option A.
  • Trust. Escrow can be coded — release on delivery confirmation. But the "confirmation" step needs a trusted oracle (a data source that bridges off-chain reality into on-chain logic), which reintroduces the centralised trust the design tried to avoid.
  • Payments. Gas fees to deploy and per-transaction.
  • Logistics. Unchanged.
  • Regulation. The merchant is now operating a custodial smart contract — under MiCA this may trigger licensing obligations she does not want.
  • Accessibility. Worst of the three. Customer must interact with the contract through a wallet and pay gas to authorise the escrow release.

Recommendation: Option B. It actually addresses the issue (cross-border payment friction) that motivated the experiment, while not introducing a new attack surface for which the merchant has no staff. It pushes the heaviest regulatory and security obligations onto the processor — where economies of scale make them tractable — and leaves the merchant doing what she is competent at: selling printers.

The general rule from Lesson 3 of Weeks 12–13 holds: ask which of the seven issues a "blockchain solution" is solving, and how. Option B uses crypto's genuine strength (cross-border value transfer without correspondent banks) without pretending it solves logistics or fraud detection.

Case 7

Case Study 7 — "The smart-contract flat-share that someone exploited"

Coverage: Weeks 12-13 (DApps Lesson 2 — Smart Contracts, Re-entrancy, Code-is-Law) · Week 10 (Applied Cryptography Lesson 2)

Scenario

Four roommates in Lavapiés deployed a RentalEscrow smart contract on a Polygon test network. It holds their €1 200 security deposit and is supposed to release it back to them at the end of the lease if the landlord does not raise a dispute within seven days. Three weeks in, someone discovers a bug: when one roommate calls withdrawShare(), the contract first sends them their share and then updates the internal accounting. An attacker has used this re-entrancy to drain not just the deposit but also the next month's rent that had pre-arrived.

Question

Explain the vulnerability and the fix in plain language. Why is "code is law" controversial in cases like this, and what does the history of Ethereum Classic (ETC) have to do with the answer?

Possible answer

The bug is the classic re-entrancy vulnerability that took down The DAO in June 2016 and remains in the top three categories of smart-contract exploit eight years later. The shape of the bug is small enough to fit on one slide.

withdrawShare() performs three steps:

  1. Check — verify the caller is entitled to a withdrawal.
  2. Effect — send Ether back to the caller.
  3. Interaction-update — set the caller's internal balance to zero.

Step 2 in Solidity uses a low-level call (call.value(...) or .transfer(...)) that hands execution control to the recipient's contract. If the recipient is a hostile contract, it can run arbitrary code in that moment — including calling withdrawShare() again. Because step 3 has not yet executed, the internal accounting still says the caller is owed money. The attacker re-enters, drains again, re-enters, drains again, in a loop. The contract empties.

The fix is the Checks-Effects-Interactions pattern. Reorder the function so that all state changes happen before any external call:

  1. Check — verify entitlement.
  2. Effect — zero out the caller's balance.
  3. Interaction — only now send the Ether.

If the recipient re-enters, the second call finds the balance is zero and refuses. The bug is closed at the cost of one line of code being moved upward.

Alternative defences:

  • Re-entrancy guard. A boolean lock set at the start of any state-mutating function and cleared at the end. Re-entry sees the lock set and aborts. OpenZeppelin ships a battle-tested ReentrancyGuard modifier.
  • Pull over push. Have users withdraw funds owed to them rather than the contract sending funds proactively. The withdrawal is a separate transaction the user initiates.

Now to the philosophical part: code is law.

The "code is law" doctrine, popularised by Lawrence Lessig and embraced by the early Ethereum community, says that whatever the deployed contract does is the legitimate outcome — because all participants agreed up front to be bound by the code, the code is public, and trying to "correct" exploits with off-chain political intervention undermines the entire trust model.

The DAO hack of June 2016 stress-tested that doctrine. About USD 60 million worth of Ether was draining out of a contract through this exact re-entrancy bug. The Ethereum community made the controversial decision to perform a hard fork — a coordinated change to the protocol that effectively rewound the chain to a state before the theft and returned funds to their previous owners.

A vocal minority refused to follow the fork on principle. "The bug was in the code; the contract did what the code said; rewriting history because we don't like the result is exactly the central-authority intervention this technology was supposed to abolish." They kept running the original, unforked chain. It still runs today as Ethereum Classic (ETC).

The lesson, transferred onto the flat-share case: legally, there is no "code is law" defence in a Spanish housing dispute — the roommates can sue in court, the smart contract is not a legally binding contract under Spanish civil law in 2026, and the courts will look at what people agreed, not at what the buggy Solidity executed. Practically, the roommates have lost real money in a real network, and the only recoverable path is a new contract deployed with the fix, plus reputation pressure on the exploiter.

The professional takeaway. Smart-contract security is its own engineering discipline. Re-entrancy, integer overflow, oracle manipulation, front-running, flash-loan attacks, signature replay, and access-control mistakes each have books written about them. Real contracts get audited by firms specialising in this work (Trail of Bits, ConsenSys Diligence, OpenZeppelin) before they hold money. The cost of an audit is a fraction of the cost of any of the historical exploits.

Case Study 8 — "Patching, segmentation, and an old printer"

Coverage: Week 09 (OS Hardening §1.2; Network Security §2.1) · Week 11 (Firewall Lesson 3; Vulnerability Scanner Lesson 7)

Scenario

A small architectural firm in Madrid has a flat office network. Eight Windows laptops, a Synology NAS (Network-Attached Storage), and a 2012 multifunction printer that prints from any device on the network. The IT consultant (you, on a Saturday) runs a quick nmap scan from your own laptop after getting written authorisation, and finds the printer is exposing a web interface with a known CVE allowing unauthenticated firmware upload.

Question

Lay out a remediation plan that addresses (a) the immediate risk, (b) the network design issue that allowed the printer to be reachable from any user device, and (c) the long-term posture so the firm catches the next such finding before you find it on a Saturday. Reference at least three concrete tools / frameworks from the course.

Possible answer

Immediate risk (this weekend).

Disconnect the printer from the network. Yes, this is rude on a Friday evening, but a 14-year-old printer with an unauthenticated firmware-upload bug is one Shodan search away from being part of a botnet. Confirm with the firm's principal that physical-USB printing is acceptable until Monday.

Pull the printer's firmware version, the CVE identifier from the NVD (National Vulnerability Database), and the vendor's security bulletin. Check whether the vendor still supports the device. For a 2012 model, the answer is probably no. End-of-life hardware that exposes the network is not a patch problem; it is a procurement problem.

Network design issue (next week).

The flat office network is the architectural problem. The principle to apply is network segmentation — slicing the LAN (Local Area Network) into VLANs (Virtual LANs) so that an IoT (Internet of Things) device cannot reach a user workstation, a user workstation cannot reach a server share by default, and so on.

For a firm this size, a reasonable target architecture is three VLANs:

  1. User VLAN — the eight laptops. Allowed to reach the Internet and the NAS via specific protocols (SMB on the NAS only).
  2. Server VLAN — the NAS. Reachable from User VLAN on SMB only; not reachable from the IoT VLAN at all.
  3. IoT VLAN — printer, smart-TV-in-the-meeting-room, the espresso machine that for some reason needed Wi-Fi. Allowed to reach the Internet on whatever it needs; not allowed to originate connections to either of the other VLANs.

The inter-VLAN rules live in a small firewall — pfSense or OPNsense on a low-power x86 box, or a small commercial router with a real ACL engine. The user prints via a queue that the User VLAN can reach; the printer never originates traffic anywhere.

Add a host-based firewall on every laptop and the NAS as a second line. The Windows Defender Firewall on the "Domain" or "Private" profile, with the default-deny inbound rule intact, blocks the printer from reaching the laptops even if the segmentation fails for some reason. Belt and braces.

Replace the 2012 printer at the next budget cycle. A new model with current firmware support is cheaper than the incident response of one bad weekend.

Long-term posture (next month and beyond).

Three controls.

  1. Vulnerability scanning, scheduled. Run an authenticated scan (Tenable Nessus, Rapid7 InsightVM, or — for a budget-conscious small firm — OpenVAS / Greenbone) against the internal network every month, and an external one against any publicly exposed services every week. Subscribe the firm's principal to a one-page email summary so they cannot pretend they did not see findings.

  2. Patch management as a process. Document a window — say, every second Tuesday from 18:00 — when patches will be applied. Windows Update for the laptops, Synology DSM for the NAS, vendor firmware for any IoT devices that survive the segmentation review. Anything that has not been patched in 90 days is flagged in the next monthly review.

  3. Asset inventory. A list of every device that has an IP address on the network, with a designated owner, a firmware/OS version, and an EOL (End-of-Life) date. The CIS Controls, Control 1, makes this the first thing every framework asks for, because you cannot defend assets you cannot list.

The reason all of this matters. The printer scan found something this Saturday because you were looking. Most of the time nobody is looking, and the printer is one of seventeen exposed devices the firm does not know it has. The architectural fix (segmentation) and the process fix (scanning + patching + inventory) turn a one-off Saturday discovery into a routine Tuesday alert.

Case 9

Case Study 9 — "GDPR, 72 hours, and a leaked customer list"

Coverage: Weeks 12-13 (E-Commerce Issue #2 — Privacy) · Week 09 (Web Security §2.2) · Week 11 (Defence-in-Depth Lesson 1)

Scenario

A small Madrid e-commerce business (handmade ceramics, ships to fifteen EU countries) discovers on a Tuesday morning that their customer database — names, e-mail addresses, shipping addresses, order history for about 4 700 customers — was downloaded by an attacker. Initial analysis points to a SQL injection vulnerability in a discount-code field on the checkout page that was not parameterised. The owner is panicked: "do I have to tell people? Whom do I call first? Will I be fined? Is the AEPD coming?"

Question

Walk the owner through the next 72 hours, citing the specific legal obligations and authorities involved. Then identify what the engineering fix is and what longer-term controls would have prevented this.

Possible answer

Calm first, action second. The 72-hour clock under the GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) starts from the moment the business becomes aware of the breach — not from the breach itself. Today, Tuesday morning, is hour zero.

Within hour 0 to hour 6: contain and document.

  • Disconnect the vulnerable application from the Internet, or at least disable the discount-code feature and any code paths that touch the affected database. The first priority is stopping the bleed.
  • Begin a written incident log. Time-stamp every action and decision. The log will be the basis of every report you are about to file.
  • Preserve evidence: web-server access logs, database query logs, EDR (if any) telemetry, the SQL-injection request itself if you can find it. Forensics teams will want this; so will the supervisory authority.

Within hour 0 to hour 24: assess the scope.

  • What data was accessed? Names, e-mail addresses, shipping addresses, order history — all of these are personal data under GDPR Article 4(1). No financial data was directly stored (the merchant uses a tokenised processor — good).
  • How many data subjects are affected? Roughly 4 700, across fifteen Member States.
  • What is the risk to those data subjects? Identity-theft potential is moderate (names, e-mail, shipping address combined). Re-identification risk is moderate. Likely category: a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, which triggers both the supervisory-authority notification (Article 33) and the data-subject notification (Article 34).

Hours 24 to 72: notify the supervisory authority.

In Spain, the supervisory authority is the AEPD (Agencia Española de Protección de Datos). The mechanism is the AEPD's electronic registry; the form requires the nature of the breach, the categories and approximate number of data subjects, the categories and approximate number of records, the contact point (the DPO — Data Protection Officer, or an equivalent), the likely consequences, and the measures taken (or proposed).

Because customers span multiple Member States, the GDPR's one-stop-shop mechanism applies: the AEPD will be the lead authority but will coordinate with the supervisory authorities of every Member State whose residents were affected — the CNIL in France, the Garante in Italy, the DSB in Austria, and so on.

The 72-hour deadline is for the initial notification. A complete picture is not required at hour 72 — what is required is the best assessment available at that moment, with a commitment to update.

Within days, not weeks: notify the data subjects.

Article 34 requires the controller to communicate the breach to affected data subjects without undue delay when the breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights and freedoms. For this case, the answer is yes. Send a clear-language e-mail explaining what happened, what data was affected, what the risks are (phishing using the e-mail addresses being the most common), and what the customer can do (be vigilant about phishing that references the shop, change any password that was reused). Do not bury this in legalese.

On the fines question. The GDPR allows fines of up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher. For a small ceramics business, the realistic outcome — assuming the breach is disclosed honestly, the cause is documented, and remediation is convincing — is a much smaller fine or a formal warning. The AEPD has historically been more punitive toward firms that concealed a breach than toward firms that handled it transparently.

The engineering fix.

SQL injection is preventable with parameterised queries (prepared statements). The discount-code field should never have been concatenated into a SQL string. In Python with psycopg2 against PostgreSQL the difference is one character — cursor.execute("SELECT ... WHERE code = %s", (user_input,)) instead of cursor.execute("SELECT ... WHERE code = '" + user_input + "'"). Every modern web framework — Django, Rails, Spring, Laravel — uses parameterised queries through its ORM (Object-Relational Mapping) by default. The reason injection still happens is that someone wrote the one query that bypasses the ORM "because it was simpler" and never came back.

Longer-term controls.

  • WAF (Web Application Firewall). A reverse proxy that filters obvious attack patterns before they reach the application. Hosted options like Cloudflare or AWS WAF can be deployed in an afternoon. A WAF would not be the last line of defence — parameterisation is — but it catches a high fraction of automated injection attempts.
  • OWASP ASVS (Application Security Verification Standard). Adopt the relevant level (Level 1 is appropriate for a small e-commerce business) and walk through it once a year. Available free at OWASP ASVS.
  • Vulnerability scanning of the web application. Automated scanners (OWASP ZAP, Burp Suite) catch a meaningful fraction of injection bugs in development.
  • Data minimisation. GDPR Article 5(1)(c). Does the shop need to retain shipping addresses three years after the order? Probably not. Define a retention schedule and delete data on schedule. A breach of data you no longer hold is not a breach.
  • Defence in depth (Week 11 Lesson 1). No single control would have saved the business. Parameterised queries + WAF + minimal retention + monitoring + a backup of the database from before the breach all add up to a posture where a single bug does not become a company-ending incident.

The AEPD will not be at the door on Wednesday morning. They will read the report and likely respond with a request for more information, a timeline, and a remediation commitment. Handle that interaction with respect and full transparency.

Case 10

Case Study 10 — "Choosing the right wireless authentication for a campus"

Coverage: Week 10 (Authentication Lesson 1) · Week 09 (Network Security §2.1) · Week 11 (Defence in Depth Lesson 1)

Scenario

A medium-sized college in Madrid is redesigning its campus Wi-Fi. Today every student and staff member uses a single shared WPA2 (Wi-Fi Protected Access 2) pre-shared key that has been pasted on the cafeteria wall since 2019. The campus IT director wants to move to "real" authentication. He is choosing between (A) keeping a shared password but rotating it every semester, (B) WPA2-Enterprise with username and password backed by RADIUS (Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service), or (C) WPA2-Enterprise with EAP-TLS (Extensible Authentication Protocol — Transport Layer Security) using per-device certificates issued from a campus PKI.

Question

Compare the three options on identity strength, manageability, user experience, and resistance to credential theft. Recommend one. Then describe what additional controls you would deploy regardless of which authentication option is chosen.

Possible answer

The three options sit at very different points on the trade-off curve.

Option A — shared password rotated each semester.

  • Identity strength. Zero. The network has no idea which user is connected. If a device misbehaves, the only logged identity is "someone who knew the password as of this semester".
  • Manageability. Easy until the moment you need to revoke access for one person — say, an expelled student. You cannot. You can only rotate the password and tell everyone to update.
  • User experience. Excellent until rotation day, when 8 000 devices stop working at 09:00 on a Monday.
  • Credential-theft resistance. Worst. The password is on the cafeteria wall, and it is on the screenshot the visiting prospective student sent home, and it is in every device's stored network list whether the device is still authorised or not.

Option B — WPA2-Enterprise + RADIUS, username + password.

  • Identity strength. Much better. Every association is tied to a specific user. The campus identity directory (Active Directory, Okta, or a campus-deployed FreeRADIUS backed by LDAP) is the source of truth.
  • Manageability. Revocation is one directory change. Onboarding is one ticket.
  • User experience. Good — type the same username/password the user already types into the campus portal.
  • Credential-theft resistance. Better than Option A, but still vulnerable to credential phishing. A student who falls for an "update your Wi-Fi password" e-mail hands the attacker network-level access. MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication) can be layered on but is awkward to enforce at the Wi-Fi association step. Also vulnerable to evil-twin attacks where a malicious access point with the same SSID prompts for the credential.

Option C — WPA2-Enterprise + EAP-TLS with per-device certificates from a campus PKI.

  • Identity strength. Highest of the three. Each device has its own certificate signed by the campus CA (Certificate Authority). The certificate is bound cryptographically to a private key held on the device — the user does not type it, cannot phish it, and cannot reuse it on an unauthorised device.
  • Manageability. Significantly more upfront work — set up the campus PKI, deploy a certificate-enrolment mechanism (SCEP — Simple Certificate Enrollment Protocol, or modern MDM-based — Mobile Device Management — enrolment), train the helpdesk on issuance and revocation. After that, day-to-day operations are easier than Option B because there is no password reset traffic.
  • User experience. Excellent after enrolment. The device just connects.
  • Credential-theft resistance. Best. No password to phish, no shared secret to leak. Lost device → revoke its certificate via OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol) or CRL (Certificate Revocation List).

Recommendation. Option C — WPA2-Enterprise with EAP-TLS — for staff and a campus-managed-device fleet (lab desktops, faculty laptops). Option B as a transitional second method for student-owned devices ("BYOD" — Bring Your Own Device) until certificate enrolment can be made painless on those devices. Option A retired entirely.

This is exactly the architecture used by eduroam, the international academic federation that links thousands of universities worldwide and that the college should join during the migration — it gives free, certificate-authenticated roaming Wi-Fi to every student visiting any participating institution worldwide.

Additional controls deployed regardless of the authentication choice.

  • Network segmentation. Three VLANs at minimum: staff, student devices, and guest. The guest VLAN reaches the Internet but not the campus network. The student VLAN reaches student-facing services and the Internet but not the staff or admin segments.
  • Captive portal for guests. Time-limited (12 hours), e-mail-verified. No shared secrets.
  • WPA3 transition where supported. WPA3 strengthens the key-agreement step (SAE — Simultaneous Authentication of Equals — replaces the older 4-way handshake) and adds forward secrecy. Older devices that only support WPA2 stay on WPA2/WPA3 mixed mode for a transition period.
  • Logging and monitoring. Every authentication event is logged. Repeated failures from one device, or association attempts during odd hours from devices that should be in a classroom, feed into the SIEM (Security Information and Event Management) and trigger alerts.
  • Annual review. Certificates rotate on a schedule. Revocation procedures are rehearsed (not just documented).

The wider lesson, tying back to Week 10. Authentication is not "one decision, made once". It is the foundation for everything else — authorisation rules, accounting / audit, incident response. Get this layer wrong and every other control in the course is downstream of compromised identity. Get it right and the rest of the security stack actually has something coherent to defend.

MCQ 19

MCQ 19 — AES-GCM properties

Coverage: Authentication Protocols, Applied Cryptography, Key Management — Lesson 2
AEAD provides three properties simultaneously.

AES-256 in GCM (Galois/Counter Mode) is an example of an AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data) cipher because it provides which of the following in a single primitive?

Answer: C.

AEAD ciphers like AES-GCM bundle three guarantees in one operation: the ciphertext cannot be read without the key (confidentiality), any tampering is detected via the authentication tag (integrity), and the tag also proves it was produced by someone who held the key (authenticity). Older modes like AES-CBC require a separate MAC to achieve the integrity/authenticity properties.

MCQ 20

MCQ 20 — Nonce reuse in AES-GCM

Coverage: Authentication Protocols, Applied Cryptography, Key Management — Lesson 2
"Number used once". What happens if you use it twice under the same key?

What is the consequence of reusing a nonce in AES-GCM with the same key?

Answer: C.

Reusing a (key, nonce) pair in GCM is one of the very few "always" rules in cryptography. The XOR of two ciphertexts produced under the same nonce reveals the XOR of the two plaintexts, and the authentication key is also exposed in a way that lets an attacker forge messages. This is why the 12-byte nonce is generated freshly with os.urandom (or equivalent) on every encryption.

In the Diffie-Hellman key exchange, an eavesdropper who observes the public values exchanged between Alice and Bob cannot easily recover their shared secret because doing so requires solving:

MCQ 39

MCQ 39 — Proof of Stake vs Proof of Work

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 1

Ethereum moved from Proof of Work (PoW) to Proof of Stake (PoS) in the September 2022 event known as "The Merge". The primary practical difference between the two is:

Answer: B.

The honest answer is that PoW and PoS are two different ways to answer the same question: who gets to propose the next block, and why should everyone else trust them? PoW answers "the one who burned the most electricity solving a puzzle". PoS answers "the one who has the most to lose if they cheat". Both use cryptography. MCQ 40

MCQ 40 — 51% attack

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 1
Majority control of what resource?

A "51% attack" on a Proof-of-Work blockchain refers to a situation where:

Answer: B.

The 51% attack is about hash-power majority on a PoW chain (or stake majority on a PoS chain). The attacker can rewrite very recent history — typically the last few blocks — which allows double-spending and selective censorship of transactions. It does not let them forge signatures, so they cannot move coins out of arbitrary accounts. Deep history is protected by the sheer cost of redoing all the proof-of-work. Smaller chains have suffered real 51% attacks: Ethereum Classic (ETC) was hit several times in 2019 and 2020.

MCQ 41

MCQ 41 — Smart contract gas

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 2
Halting Problem, with money.

On Ethereum, the concept of "gas" exists primarily because:

Answer: A.

The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) is Turing-complete. Without a cost on each operation, a malicious or buggy contract could run an infinite loop and freeze the network. Gas is the answer: every opcode has a fixed gas cost; transactions specify a maximum (gas limit); if the contract runs out of gas, execution halts and the user still pays for the work done. EIP-1559 (2021) split gas into a base fee that is burned and a priority tip paid to the validator.

SECTION E — Short Essay Questions

Essay 1

Essay 1 — The CIA Triad as a design discipline

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 1, §1.1
Define each term, then trade off two of them.

Explain the CIA Triad and give one concrete example of a real-world security control for each pillar. Then describe a situation in which two of the three pillars come into tension, and how a system designer might resolve it.

Model answer.

Confidentiality means that information is disclosed only to those authorised to see it (example: full-disk encryption on a laptop using BitLocker or LUKS). Integrity means that information cannot be altered without detection (example: a digital signature on a software update, or a Git commit hash chain). Availability means that information and services are accessible when needed (example: a redundant database cluster and a tested backup-restore procedure). The classical tension is between confidentiality and availability — encrypting every backup with a single key concentrates risk: lose the key and the data is unavailable; share the key widely and confidentiality erodes. A reasonable resolution is to use HSMs or a key-management service such as AWS KMS with split-knowledge / dual-control procedures and a documented break-glass process, sized to the organisation's recovery point and recovery time objectives. Integrity sometimes pulls against availability as well: a strict signature check that refuses to boot a tampered system protects integrity but reduces availability for the user. The discipline lies in knowing which of the three pillars matters most for the specific data and accepting the trade-off explicitly rather than by accident.

Essay 2

Essay 2 — The principle of least privilege

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 1, §1.1–§1.2
User accounts, services, applications, all the same idea.

Define the principle of least privilege. Give two examples of how it is enforced inside a modern operating system, and one example of how it is enforced in a network or cloud setting.

Model answer.

Least privilege says that every subject (user, service, process) should hold exactly the rights necessary to perform its task — and no more. Inside an operating system, the principle is enforced by user accounts and groups (a developer does not log in as root for daily work), by Mandatory Access Control frameworks such as SELinux or AppArmor that confine even the root account, and by user-account-control or sudo prompts that require explicit elevation. In the cloud, least privilege appears as IAM (Identity and Access Management) policies in AWS IAM, Google Cloud IAM, or Azure RBAC; the same principle drives Kubernetes RBAC and database role design. The hotel analogy used in class makes this concrete: the cleaner's keycard opens the corridor and the rooms in their list during their shift only, not the safe room or the management floor. When breach analysis points to "the compromised account had administrator rights it did not need" — Capital One 2019, Equifax 2017, and many others — the diagnosis is a least-privilege failure.

Essay 3

Essay 3 — The Cyber Kill Chain and the MITRE ATT&CK framework

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 1, §1.3
Linear chain vs matrix of TTPs.

Compare the Cyber Kill Chain with the MITRE ATT&CK framework. When would a defender reach for each?

Model answer.

The Cyber Kill Chain, formulated by Lockheed Martin, is a seven-stage linear model: Reconnaissance, Weaponisation, Delivery, Exploitation, Installation, Command and Control, and Actions on Objectives. Its strength is the executive-friendly narrative; its weakness is the linearity, since real intrusions loop, branch, and skip stages. MITRE ATT&CK is a publicly maintained matrix of Tactics (the "why"), Techniques (the "how" in general), and Procedures (the "how" in concrete tool-and-implementation terms), drawn from observed adversary behaviour. ATT&CK is the daily working language of SOC analysts and threat-hunters because it lets them say "we have detection coverage for T1059.001 (PowerShell) but not for T1098 (Account Manipulation)" with the granularity that drives backlog work. A useful pairing in practice is the Kill Chain for the executive summary slide ("the attacker reached Stage 5 of 7 before we stopped them") and ATT&CK for the technical retrospective and detection-engineering roadmap.

Essay 4

Essay 4 — The OSI model as a security map

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 2, §2.1
For each layer, one attack and one control.

Walk through the OSI model and, for at least four layers, name one attack and one control associated with that layer.

Model answer.

Layer 1 (Physical): cable tapping, rogue access points — controlled by physical security, secure wiring, and authenticated cabinets. Layer 2 (Data Link): ARP spoofing, MAC flooding, rogue DHCP — controlled by 802.1X, Dynamic ARP Inspection, port security, and DHCP snooping. Layer 3 (Network): IP spoofing, routing attacks — controlled by firewall rules on source/destination IP and by IPsec for authenticated, encrypted transport. Layer 4 (Transport): TCP SYN floods, port scans — controlled by stateful firewalls, SYN cookies, and rate limiting. Layer 7 (Application): the OWASP Top 10 — controlled by a Web Application Firewall, secure coding practices (output encoding, parameterised queries), and a strong Content Security Policy. The point of the OSI map is that "network security" is not one thing; it is a stack of independent controls at successive layers, which is the practical meaning of defence in depth.

Essay 5

Essay 5 — Firewall design philosophy

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 2, §2.2
Default deny, segmentation, documentation.

You have been asked to write a one-page "firewall design philosophy" for a small organisation moving from a single flat network to a segmented one. Outline the principles you would include.

Model answer.

Start with default deny: nothing crosses a boundary unless an explicit rule permits it. Document every rule with a business purpose, an owner, and a review date. Segment by trust zone, not by department: a "user workstation" zone, a "server" zone (with sub-zones if regulation requires it, for example a PCI scope), a "management" zone, and a guest/IoT zone. Permit only the minimum service ports between zones — RDP (port 3389) and SSH should not be reachable from the user zone except through a bastion or VPN. Log denials; alert on patterns of denials. Review the rule set quarterly: stale rules are how breaches keep happening years after the original employee resigned. Use named groups and aliases (pfSense, OPNsense) so that the rule reads like English. Treat the firewall as code where possible — version-controlled exports, peer-reviewed changes, automated tests where supported. And remember the deep truth: the firewall is one of many controls, not the entire perimeter, and the perimeter is not the only place where filtering matters.

Essay 6

Essay 6 — The OWASP Top 10 in your own words

Coverage: OS, Network and Web Security and Malware — Lesson 2, §2.3
Pick three categories you know well.

Choose three categories from the current OWASP Top 10 and, in your own words, explain what each category means, give one realistic example, and name one defence.

Model answer.

Broken Access Control — the most common category, covering everything from "regular user can call admin-only endpoint by typing the URL" to insecure direct object references ("/api/invoices/12345" returns someone else's invoice). Defence: authorise every action server-side based on the authenticated identity and never trust client-supplied object identifiers without checking ownership. Cryptographic Failures — using weak hashes (MD5, SHA-1 for passwords), no encryption in transit, or mismanaging keys. Defence: TLS 1.3 everywhere, AEAD modes for application-level encryption, and password storage with Argon2id or PBKDF2 at 600,000+ iterations as recommended by the OWASP Password Storage Cheat Sheet. Injection — chiefly SQL injection, command injection, LDAP injection, and similar. Defence: parameterised queries with bound variables, allowlisted column names where the input space is small, least-privilege database accounts, and never assemble a query by concatenating strings with user input.

Essay 7

Essay 7 — Authentication, Authorisation, Accounting (AAA)

Coverage: Auth Protocols, Applied Cryptography and Key Management — Lesson 1
Three distinct ideas, often blurred.

Distinguish authentication, authorisation, and accounting (AAA). Why do students (and many engineers) confuse the first two? Give an example of each in the context of a corporate VPN or Wi-Fi.

Model answer.

Authentication answers "who are you, and can you prove it?". Authorisation answers "given that you are who you claim to be, what are you allowed to do?". Accounting answers "what did you actually do, when, from where?". They are confused because in many simple systems they collapse into one step ("I logged in, therefore I can see everything"), and because tools often combine them. On a corporate VPN or 802.1X Wi-Fi network, authentication might use the user's credentials plus a hardware token (FIDO2/WebAuthn) brokered by a RADIUS server. Authorisation might place the user into a specific VLAN with specific firewall policies based on their group membership. Accounting records the session start, the bytes transferred, and the session end, feeding a SIEM. When an incident hits, the accounting record is what the responder reads first. The discipline is to keep the three roles separate in design even when a single product (e.g., a NAC platform) handles all three.

Essay 8

Essay 8 — The TLS 1.3 handshake at a friendly level of detail

Coverage: Auth Protocols, Applied Cryptography and Key Management — Lesson 2
ClientHello, key share, certificate, finished.

Describe the steps of a TLS 1.3 handshake at a level a first-year student can defend at the board. Emphasise which step provides which property (authenticity, confidentiality, forward secrecy).

Model answer.

The client opens with a ClientHello containing supported cipher suites, supported groups for Diffie-Hellman, and an Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) key share. The server responds with a ServerHello, its own ECDH key share, and a certificate chain signed by a Certificate Authority trusted by the client. From the two ECDH key shares both sides derive a shared secret that no passive observer can compute, even with full transcript. The server signs the handshake transcript with its certificate's private key, proving control of the certificate and therefore — given a working PKI and CT logs — its identity. A Finished message on each side confirms that both parties derived the same keys and that no man-in-the-middle altered the transcript. After this, traffic flows under an AEAD cipher such as AES-GCM or ChaCha20-Poly1305. Because the ECDH ephemeral keys are discarded after the session, recording today's encrypted traffic and breaking the server's long-term key later does not reveal the session — this is forward secrecy.

Essay 9

Essay 9 — Key management as a lifecycle

Coverage: Auth Protocols, Applied Cryptography and Key Management — Lesson 3
Seven stages, and why each matters.

Walk through the cryptographic key lifecycle from NIST SP 800-57. For each stage, give one risk that arises if the stage is mishandled.

Model answer.

Generation — a weak random number generator at this stage compromises every key derived afterwards. Distribution — emailing the key to a colleague is the most common informal violation; the right channel is a key-management service or an HSM-backed protocol. Storage — keys in source code repositories on GitHub are the modern equivalent of writing the password on a sticky note (and bots are scanning for them within minutes; the GitHub secret-scanning feature exists for this reason). Use — using the same key beyond its cryptoperiod or in a mode it was not designed for. Rotation / change — long-lived keys magnify the blast radius of a compromise. Archive — old keys that decrypt old ciphertext must be kept somewhere, but kept separately from current operational keys. Revocation and destruction — failing to zeroise material on key compromise leaves a window the attacker uses. Modern services such as AWS KMS, Google Cloud KMS, and Azure Key Vault automate most of these stages, but the policy decisions — who can use which key, how long, with what logging — remain the engineer's.

Essay 10

Essay 10 — Why FIDO2/WebAuthn is phishing-resistant

Coverage: Auth Protocols, Applied Cryptography and Key Management — Lesson 1
Origin binding, public-key challenge.

Explain in your own words why a FIDO2 / WebAuthn hardware authenticator is considered phishing-resistant while a TOTP code from an authenticator app is not.

Model answer.

A TOTP code is a six-digit number that a user can type into any form. A real-time phishing proxy (such as Modlishka or Evilginx) impersonates the legitimate site, asks the user for the code, and relays it within the 30-second window before it expires. The TOTP code does not know which site is asking. FIDO2/WebAuthn flips the model. The authenticator stores a public-key pair per origin. When a site challenges the user, the browser tells the authenticator which origin it actually loaded (e.g., https://www.bank.com). The authenticator signs the challenge with the private key bound to that origin. If the user has been phished and the page actually loaded from https://www.bank-secure-login.com, the authenticator will either refuse to sign or will produce a signature against the wrong origin, which the real bank's server rejects. The cryptographic binding to the origin is what removes the user's eyes from the chain of trust — they can no longer be the weak link. Passkeys are this same model with synchronisation, removing the loss-of-device objection.

Essay 11

Essay 11 — Defence in Depth in a startup that just opened an office

Coverage: Computer Security Software — Lesson 1
People, process, technology, all layers.

A friend asks you to sketch a defence-in-depth posture for a 25-person startup that has just rented its first office and bought 30 laptops. Outline what you would put in place in the first month, organised by layer.

Model answer.

Endpoints: full-disk encryption with BitLocker or FileVault, an EDR (Microsoft Defender for Endpoint or SentinelOne), a host firewall enabled and configured, automatic OS and browser patching. Identity: SSO (Single Sign-On) with a reputable IdP and MFA enforced with FIDO2 keys or platform authenticators; passkeys where supported; a password manager deployed and required for any service not covered by SSO. Network: a small OPNsense or vendor firewall at the office, guest Wi-Fi isolated from the corporate VLAN, no inbound services exposed to the Internet. Logging: ship endpoint and firewall logs to a SIEM (Wazuh on the budget end, Microsoft Sentinel or Elastic higher up). Backups: tested 3-2-1 backups for SaaS and laptops, encrypted, with offline copies and restore drills on the calendar. People: a one-hour onboarding on phishing, password managers, and what to do if a laptop is lost; a clear "report don't hide" policy. Process: a written incident-response runbook (even a one-pager), a list of who to call (and a printed copy, in case email is down), and a quarterly review.

Essay 12

Essay 12 — How an EDR catches what AV misses

Coverage: Computer Security Software — Lesson 5
Telemetry, behaviour, response.

Explain the conceptual difference between a legacy antivirus and a modern EDR. Use a scenario such as a phishing email containing a macro-enabled document.

Model answer.

A legacy antivirus scans the document file against a database of known-malicious signatures. If the macro is freshly written and signed under a stolen certificate, the signature lookup returns clean and the file passes. A modern EDR — for example CrowdStrike Falcon, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, or SentinelOne — records what the document then does: Word spawning PowerShell, PowerShell making an outbound HTTPS connection to an unfamiliar domain, that connection downloading a binary, the binary writing into a startup folder, an attempt to escalate privilege via a known technique mapped to MITRE ATT&CK. Each step alone is suspicious; the chain is unmistakable. The EDR raises a detection mapped to ATT&CK techniques, isolates the host, and lets the analyst replay the whole tree of events. Even if no individual artefact has a matching signature, the behaviour matches. This is also why XDR extends the same idea across email, identity, and network telemetry.

Essay 13

Essay 13 — IDS vs IPS, and when each is appropriate

Coverage: Computer Security Software — Lesson 4
False positives, fail-open vs fail-closed, audit vs enforcement.

Compare an IDS and an IPS. Give a scenario where you would deploy each, and explain how you would tune the false-positive rate.

Model answer.

An IDS observes traffic out-of-band (usually via a SPAN/mirror port) and raises alerts. An IPS sits in-line and can drop or reset matching traffic. The difference matters most when a rule fires incorrectly: an IDS false positive is an annoyance; an IPS false positive cuts a legitimate connection in production. A reasonable rule of thumb is to deploy Snort or Suricata in IDS mode first, monitor for two to four weeks, identify the high-confidence rules, and only then promote those specific rules to blocking. Newer or noisier rules continue in detect-only mode. Zeek is often added alongside to produce rich connection metadata for hunting and correlation. The tuning loop is: false positive → analyst review → suppress or modify the rule (or fix the underlying configuration that triggered it). Tuning is the hard part of network detection engineering; the box is not the work.

Essay 14

Essay 14 — Password storage in 2026

Coverage: Computer Security Software — Lesson 6
Salt, slow, memory-hard.

A junior developer asks "what should we use to hash user passwords for our new application?". Write the answer you would give them.

Model answer.

Use Argon2id with the parameters recommended in the OWASP Password Storage Cheat Sheet — at current writing, memory cost around 19 MiB, 2 iterations, 1 degree of parallelism — and adjust upward if hardware allows. If the platform does not have a vetted Argon2id implementation, fall back to scrypt or PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA256 with 600,000+ iterations. Always salt per user (the KDF does this for you if you pass a fresh salt). Store the algorithm identifier and parameters alongside the hash so that you can migrate later without breaking existing accounts. Do not write your own KDF. Do not use plain SHA-256 or MD5 — they are designed to be fast, which is the opposite of what you need. Do not reverse-encrypt the password to "let the helpdesk read it back" — that means a leak of the database is a leak of every password. And rate-limit login attempts so that the slow hash matters less to a legitimate user and more to the attacker.

Essay 15

Essay 15 — Vulnerability management as a programme, not an event

Coverage: Computer Security Software — Lesson 7
Scan, prioritise, patch, verify, repeat.

You inherit a network of about 200 servers and 600 endpoints with no formal vulnerability-management programme. Outline how you would build one over the first three months.

Model answer.

Month 1: discover everything. Run nmap sweeps to confirm asset inventory against the CMDB. Stand up a scanner — Greenbone / OpenVAS for budget, Tenable Nessus, Rapid7 InsightVM, or Qualys VMDR for commercial. Run authenticated scans (credentials greatly improve accuracy and cut false positives). Month 2: prioritise. Triage by CVSS base score and by exploit availability (CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue is a much better real-world filter than raw CVSS). Group by service owner. Agree SLAs: critical externally exposed in 7 days, high in 30, medium in 60, low quarterly. Month 3: patch and verify. Patch in waves with rollback plans; rescan to verify; report metrics weekly to leadership; feed exceptions and risk acceptances into a register with an expiry date. By the end of the quarter the programme should produce a downward-trending chart and a known list of risk-accepted items rather than a pile of unread PDFs.

Essay 16

Essay 16 — The Bitcoin paper, in plain language

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 1
Trustless, double-spend, longest chain.

Summarise, in plain language, what the Bitcoin paper actually proposes and why it is considered a meaningful technical contribution rather than only a financial one.

Model answer.

The Bitcoin paper, published under the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto in October 2008, proposes an electronic cash system in which two parties can transact directly over the Internet without trusting any third party. The technical contribution is the solution to the double-spending problem in a setting with no trusted ledger keeper. The paper combines four ingredients: a peer-to-peer network of nodes, a chain of blocks each cryptographically tied to the previous one through a hash, a proof-of-work mechanism that makes block production expensive (so that rewriting history requires redoing all the work), and an economic incentive (the block reward and transaction fees) that aligns the rational behaviour of node operators with the health of the network. The "longest valid chain" rule lets the network converge even when nodes briefly disagree. None of the individual components is new — Merkle trees, hash functions, distributed networks, computational puzzles for spam control all pre-date the paper — but the combination is. The financial implications are downstream of the technical one: a working, public, leaderless ledger.

Essay 17

Essay 17 — Why smart-contract auditing exists as a profession

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 2
Code is law, immutability, financial value at stake.

Explain why smart-contract auditing has become a serious profession (firms like OpenZeppelin, Trail of Bits, and platforms like Immunefi) and why the typical bug-rate on a smart contract is treated very differently from the same bug rate in, say, a marketing website.

Model answer.

A deployed smart contract on a public chain is, by design, hard to change. The bytecode is on-chain and immutable unless the contract itself supports an upgrade path — and upgrade paths themselves introduce new risk (admin keys, proxy patterns). A vulnerable contract often controls significant funds: total value locked in DeFi peaked above $180 billion in 2021 and remains substantial. Once the bug is found, the attacker can drain funds in a single transaction broadcast from anywhere in the world, in seconds, with no possibility of "rolling back" the database the way a traditional web application would. The June 2016 DAO hack ($60M at the time) is the canonical example. A bug rate of "one severity-1 issue per thousand lines of code" is normal for ordinary software and survivable; on a contract holding $100M, it is fatal. The economics therefore support specialised auditing firms, formal verification, fuzzing tools, bug-bounty platforms, and the deliberate adoption of audited libraries (the OpenZeppelin Contracts library is to Solidity what libcrypto is to C — "do not roll your own").

Essay 18

Essay 18 — Stablecoins, MiCA, and the runway to redemption

Coverage: Blockchain, Decentralised Apps and E-Commerce — Lesson 3
Reserves, redemption, supervisor, run risk.

Explain what stablecoins are, why regulators in the EU have focused on them, and what MiCA requires of an Electronic Money Token issuer such as Circle for USDC.

Model answer.

A stablecoin is a crypto-asset whose value is pegged to a reference asset, typically a fiat currency. The technology piece — a token on Ethereum or Solana redeemable at par for a dollar or a euro — is straightforward; the hard problem is the backing. If the issuer's reserves are not liquid, not segregated, not audited, and not redeemable in full at any time, the construction looks like a money-market fund without the regulation, and it is exposed to bank-run dynamics. May 2022 (TerraUSD) and the period around March 2023 (regional-bank exposure of issuer reserves) made the point in public. MiCA, applicable in full from 30 December 2024, requires an EMT issuer to be authorised as a credit institution or e-money institution, to hold a 1:1 reserve in safe and liquid assets segregated from the issuer's own balance sheet, to publish a detailed white paper, and to grant holders a redemption right at par value at any time. Supervisors include national competent authorities, the EBA, and ESMA. Asset-Referenced Tokens (ART) have a parallel regime. The intent is that the stablecoin user can in fact get their euro back when they ask, which is the only definition of "stable" that matters.

Essay 19 — Persistent e-commerce challenges

Essay 20

Essay 20 — Where security ends and ethics begins

Coverage: All weeks — synthesis and disclaimer cross-reference
Authorisation, intent, lab vs production.

Discuss the relationship between technical security knowledge and personal responsibility. Why is the line of authorisation considered the most important single concept for a security student to internalise?

Model answer.

Every technique covered in this course can be used to defend or to attack. The technical content of "scan a host for open ports" is the same whether the student is securing their own home lab or probing a stranger's webshop. What changes is authorisation: the explicit, written permission of the system owner to perform the activity. Without it, the activity is a criminal offence in every jurisdiction covered in the course materials — Ley Orgánica 10/1995 in Spain (article 197 bis), Directive 2013/40/EU across the European Union, section 342.1 of the Canadian Criminal Code, and the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (18 U.S.C. § 1030) in the United States. Intent, age, curiosity, and the use of anonymisation tools do not change this. The mature professional path is to practise on owned infrastructure (a VirtualBox lab is plenty for most of the techniques in this course), on deliberately vulnerable applications maintained by OWASP, on CTF platforms such as Hack The Box and TryHackMe, and within written scopes — through formal employment, through a sanctioned bug-bounty programme on HackerOne, Bugcrowd, or Immunefi, or through coordinated vulnerability disclosure with the vendor. The technical skill is the easy part. The discipline to leave systems alone unless authorised is the part that makes a career possible. Security knowledge carries responsibility — that is the single thought to carry out of the course.
